\begin{abstract}
We present a physics-driven framework for heterogeneous impedance
networks in which all emergent behaviour derives from a single
variational principle: the Minimum Reflection Principle~(MRP),
$A[\Gamma] = \int \sum_{ij} \Gamma_{ij}^2\,dt \to \min$.
Three physical laws govern the system:
energy conservation~(C1: $\Gamma^2 + T = 1$),
impedance remodeling~(C2: $\Delta Z = -\eta\,\Gamma\,x_{\text{in}}\,x_{\text{out}}$),
and impedance-tagged transport~(C3).

\textbf{Part~A} proves the \emph{Dimensional Cost Irreducibility
Theorem}: for networks with heterogeneous mode counts $\{K_i\}$,
the action decomposes as $A = \Aimp(t) + \Acut$,
where $\Aimp \to 0$ under C2 learning while
$\Acut = \sum_{\text{edges}} (K_{\text{src}} - K_{\text{tgt}})^+$
has $\nabla_{\mathbf{Z}} \Acut \equiv 0$---zero gradient with
respect to all impedance variables.
Numerical experiments confirm convergence time
$\tau_{\text{conv}} \sim N^{-1.12}$ ($R^2 = 0.93$)
and a soft-cutoff parameter $\gamma$ that tunes the K-space
fractal dimension $D_K = 0.76\gamma + 0.78$,
placing biologically realistic networks in the cortical band
$D_K \in [1.3, 1.5]$.

\textbf{Part~B} gives $\Acut$ a definite geometric structure.
For a self-similar relay hierarchy in $d$ dimensions,
$\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}$---for $d = 3$,
$\Acut \propto M^{2/3}$.
The Murray-optimal bifurcation ratio is $\beta = n^{1/d}$;
for binary branching in 3D, $\beta = 2^{1/3} \approx 1.26$.
When two space-filling trees (vascular + neural) share one
volume, the packing overhead
$\Omega = B_{\text{total}}/B_{\text{tissue}} \approx 1.25$--$1.33$
quantifies the metabolic tax of dual-network life.
Combined with capillary invariance, MRP recovers Kleiber's
allometric law $B \propto M^{3/4}$.
In the notation of the title: each network is topologically
one-dimensional, but $1 \times 1 = \Omega \neq 1$.

\end{abstract}

\part*{Part A: Dimensional Cost Irreducibility}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

The nervous system couples neurons with vastly different mode
counts: cortical pyramidal cells carry $K \approx 5$ independent
impedance modes (soma, basal dendrites, apical tuft, axon,
spine neck), while unmyelinated C-fibers carry $K = 1$.
Classical models---from the scalar conductance of Hodgkin and
Huxley~\cite{HH1952} to the mean-field neural masses of
Breakspear~\cite{Breakspear2017}---sidestep this heterogeneity
by projecting all signals onto a single scalar variable.
Here we ask: what is the \emph{minimum physical cost} of coupling
such dimensionally mismatched elements?

The answer is unexpectedly rigid.
For any edge connecting a $K_i$-mode source to a $K_j$-mode
target with $K_i > K_j$, the $(K_i - K_j)$ excess modes are
totally reflected ($\gamma_k = 1$), contributing a fixed cost
$\Acut = K_i - K_j$ invariant under all impedance update
rules---including C2 impedance remodeling.
We call this the \emph{Dimensional Cost Irreducibility Theorem}
(Sec.~\ref{sec:theorem}).

The theorem has a striking corollary: hierarchical topology
with relay nodes of intermediate $K$ is a \emph{thermodynamic
necessity}---the only mechanism to reduce signal loss without
violating the physical constraints (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).

But the irreducibility theorem only proves $\Acut > 0$.
Part~B asks: \emph{how big} is $\Acut$?
By combining the theorem with space-filling and Murray-optimal
branching constraints, we show that $\Acut$ is a fractal
integral scaling as $M^{(d-1)/d}$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:acut-fractal}).
When two space-filling trees---vascular and neural---share one
volume, the packing overhead $\Omega > 1$ quantifies the
metabolic tax of dual-network life
(Sec.~\ref{sec:dual}), and MRP recovers Kleiber's allometric
law $B \propto M^{3/4}$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:single-scaling}).


% ============================================================
\section{Physical Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Network Definition}

A $\Gamma$-network consists of $N$ nodes, each carrying a
$K_i$-dimensional impedance vector
$\mathbf{Z}_i \in \mathbb{R}^{K_i}_{>0}$.
For an edge $i \to j$, the per-mode reflection coefficient is
\begin{equation}
\gamma_k = \frac{Z_{j,k} - Z_{i,k}}{Z_{j,k} + Z_{i,k}},
\quad k = 1, \ldots, \Kcom,
\label{eq:gamma}
\end{equation}
where $\Kcom = \min(K_i, K_j)$.
Excess source modes $k > \Kcom$ are totally reflected:
$\gamma_k = 1$.
This is the electromagnetic boundary condition for a waveguide
whose $k$-th mode has no counterpart in the target medium.

\subsection{Three Physical Laws}
\label{sec:laws}

\textbf{C1---Energy Conservation.}
At every edge, every tick, reflected and transmitted power sum
to incident power:
\begin{equation}
\gamma_k^2 + T_k = 1, \quad \forall\, k, \; \forall\, (i,j).
\label{eq:C1}
\end{equation}

\textbf{C2---Impedance Remodeling.}
Each node adjusts its impedance to reduce local reflection:
\begin{align}
\Delta Z_{i,k} &= +\eta \, \gamma_k \, x_{\text{in},k}\, x_{\text{out},k},
\label{eq:C2a} \\
\Delta Z_{j,k} &= -\eta \, \gamma_k \, x_{\text{in},k}\, x_{\text{out},k},
\label{eq:C2b}
\end{align}
where $x_{\text{in},k}$ is the input activity (at node $i$,
the source) and $x_{\text{out},k}$ the output activity
(at node $j$, the target) for mode~$k$; $\eta$ is the
remodeling rate.
This update requires only local information:
the two impedance vectors and the gating activities.
The term ``impedance remodeling'' generalises C2 beyond
neural synapses: Paper~5~\cite{Paper5} shows this law is
equivalent to Wolff's Law (bone), Glagov remodeling
(arteries), immune memory, and thirteen other
tissue-specific rules.

\textbf{C3---Impedance-Tagged Transport.}
Every signal carries its source impedance as metadata, enabling
downstream nodes to compute $\gamma_k$ locally without global
knowledge.

\subsection{Variational Principle}
\label{sec:action}

The Minimum Reflection Principle states that the system minimises
the total reflection action:
\begin{equation}
A[\Gamma]
= \sum_{(i,j) \in \mathcal{E}}
  \sum_{k=1}^{\Kcom(i,j)} \gamma_k^2.
\label{eq:action}
\end{equation}
The C2 update rule implements steepest descent on the
\emph{learnable} part of $A$:
$\dot{\mathbf{Z}} \propto -\nabla_{\mathbf{Z}} \Aimp$.


% ============================================================
\section{Dimensional Cost Irreducibility Theorem}
\label{sec:theorem}
% ============================================================

\begin{theorem}[Dimensional Cost Irreducibility]
\label{thm:irreducibility}
For any $\Gamma$-network with heterogeneous mode counts
$\{K_i\}$, the total reflection action decomposes as
\begin{equation}
A = \Aimp(t) + \Acut,
\label{eq:decomposition}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{enumerate}
\item
$\Aimp(t) = \sum_{(i,j)} \sum_{k=1}^{\Kcom} \gamma_k^2$
is the \emph{impedance mismatch action} over shared modes.
Under C2 dynamics, $\Aimp(t) \to 0$ as $t \to \infty$.

\item
$\Acut = \sum_{(i,j)} (K_i - K_j)^+$
is the \emph{cutoff action} from excess modes.
$\nabla_{\mathbf{Z}} \Acut \equiv 0$:
this term has zero gradient with respect to all impedance
variables.
\end{enumerate}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
For an edge $i \to j$ with $K_i > K_j$:
\begin{itemize}
\item
Modes $k \le \Kcom = K_j$:
$\gamma_k$ depends on $(Z_{i,k}, Z_{j,k})$ via
Eq.~\eqref{eq:gamma}.
C2 adjusts both impedances, driving $\gamma_k \to 0$.
These modes contribute to $\Aimp$.

\item
Modes $K_j < k \le K_i$:
the target has no mode~$k$, so $\gamma_k \equiv 1$ identically,
independent of any impedance value.
These modes contribute $(K_i - K_j)$ to $\Acut$.
Since $\gamma_k = 1$ is a \emph{topological constant}
(depending only on the existence, not the value, of modes),
$\partial \gamma_k^2 / \partial Z = 0$ for all~$Z$.
\end{itemize}
Summing over all edges completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}
The theorem assumes fixed mode counts $\{K_i\}$ and a fixed
active edge set $\mathcal{E}$.
If mode counts change dynamically (e.g., dendritic spine
growth increasing $K_j$), or if edge sprouting/pruning alters
$\mathcal{E}$, then the \emph{value} of $\Acut$ may change---but
this change is driven by topology, not by impedance gradients.
\end{remark}

\begin{corollary}[Thermodynamic Necessity of Relay Nodes]
\label{cor:relay}
A direct edge $K_{\text{src}} \to K_{\text{tgt}}$ with
$K_{\text{src}} > K_{\text{tgt}}$ totally reflects the
$(K_{\text{src}} - K_{\text{tgt}})$ excess modes.
Inserting a relay node with
$K_{\text{tgt}} < \Krelay < K_{\text{src}}$ leaves $\Acut$
unchanged but converts \emph{total signal loss} into
\emph{partial signal recovery}:
modes $K_{\text{tgt}} < k \le \Krelay$, previously
totally reflected, are now transmitted on the first hop
and can undergo C2 matching ($\gamma_k \to 0$, $T_k \to 1$).
Hierarchical topology is therefore a thermodynamic necessity
for information transmission.
\end{corollary}

\section{Numerical Results}
\label{sec:results}

All simulations use six tissue types with mode counts
$K \in \{1, 2, 3, 5\}$~\cite{ALICE_code},
initial connectivity $p_0 = 0.15$, learning rate
$\eta = 0.02$, and hard cutoff $\Delta K_{\max} = 2$
unless otherwise stated.

\subsection{Convergence and the Irreducibility Theorem}
\label{sec:convergence}

Figure~\ref{fig:action} shows the action decomposition for a
$N = 64$ network evolved for 1000 ticks.
$\Aimp$ decays exponentially with time constant
$\tau \approx 15$ ticks, reaching $\Aimp = 0$
(to machine precision) by $t \approx 170$.
$\Acut$ increases monotonically in the early phase due to
spontaneous edge sprouting, then saturates at
$\Acut \approx 1386$ for $t > 500$.
This does not contradict the theorem (which states
$\nabla_{\mathbf{Z}} \Acut \equiv 0$ at fixed topology);
the growth reflects topological exploration, while saturation
reveals a natural geometric cost ceiling.

\subsection{Scaling Law: $\tau_{\text{conv}} \sim N^{\alpha}$}
\label{sec:scaling}

We measure $\tau_{\text{conv}}$ (ticks until
$\Aimp < 0.01 \cdot \Aimp(t{=}5)$) across
$N \in \{16, 32, 64, 128\}$ with 5 trials per size.
A power-law fit yields
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\text{conv}} = C \cdot N^{\alpha},
\quad \alpha = -1.12 \pm 0.12,
\quad R^2 = 0.93.
\label{eq:scaling}
\end{equation}
The \emph{negative} exponent means larger networks converge
\emph{faster}---a non-trivial prediction.
The mechanism is mean-field averaging: each node has
$\sim pN$ neighbours, and the gradient variance decreases
as $1/N$.

\subsection{Soft Cutoff and K-Space Fractal Dimension}
\label{sec:fractal}

To introduce fractal connectivity beyond the hard cutoff,
we define a soft-cutoff probability:
\begin{equation}
p(\Delta K) = (\Delta K + 1)^{-\gamma},
\label{eq:softcutoff}
\end{equation}
where $\gamma \ge 0$ is the decay exponent.
The relationship is
\begin{equation}
D_K = 0.76\gamma + 0.78,
\label{eq:dk_fit}
\end{equation}
with the offset $\delta_{\text{C2}} \approx 0.66$ arising
from C2's intrinsic dimensional preference:
same-$K$ edges have more shared modes for gradient descent,
creating stronger impedance consensus and thus higher
survival probability under edge pruning.

Biologically, cortical fractal dimensions lie in
$D \in [1.3, 1.5]$~\cite{cortex_fractal}.
Equation~\eqref{eq:dk_fit} predicts
$\gamma \approx 0.7$--$0.9$---a narrow, falsifiable prediction.

\subsection{Joints as Multi-Layer Impedance Transformers}
\label{sec:joint}

Corollary~\ref{cor:relay} receives a striking musculoskeletal
confirmation.
An articular joint couples bone
($Z_{\text{bone}} \approx 120\;\Omega$) to synovial fluid
($Z_{\text{syn}} \approx 50\;\Omega$).
A direct interface would produce
$\Gamma = (120 - 50)/(120 + 50) = 0.538$, yielding
$T = 1 - \Gamma^2 = 0.71$---nearly 30\% signal loss.

Biological joints insert \emph{cartilage} as a graded
impedance relay:
$Z_{\text{cart}} \approx 80\;\Omega
\approx \sqrt{Z_{\text{bone}}\,Z_{\text{syn}}} = 77.5\;\Omega$,
the geometric mean that minimises reflection in a
quarter-wave transformer~\cite{Pozar2011}.
The five-layer stack
Bone$\to$Cartilage$\to$Synovial$\to$Cartilage$\to$Bone
achieves $T_{\text{total}} > 0.80$:
each interface carries $\Gamma^2 < 0.10$, and C1 holds at
every boundary.

When cartilage degrades (osteoarthritis, $\eta \approx 0$),
the joint reverts toward bone-on-bone ($T$ drops sharply)---the
relay node is physically removed, and $\Acut$ is no longer
mitigated.


\subsection{Cardiovascular Tissue Topology}
\label{sec:cv-topo}

To verify the irreducibility theorem on a biologically
grounded topology (rather than random graphs), we construct a
cardiovascular tissue blueprint: 11~\texttt{GammaNode}s
with anatomy-appropriate \texttt{TissueType}s, wired
according to the heart--aorta--periphery transmission line.

The dimensional hierarchy is:
Cardiac Purkinje ($K = 4$: SA node, AV node, ventricles,
valves, aorta, coronary) $\to$
vascular smooth muscle ($K = 2$: arterioles, pulmonary artery)
$\to$ endothelial ($K = 1$: capillaries).
This cascade reflects the physical loss of pulsatile modes
as blood flows from elastic arteries (pressure + flow in
multiple harmonic modes) to thin resistance vessels
(steady-state Poiseuille flow).

Running 300~ticks with cortical stimulus
($\eta = 0.02$, seed~$= 42$):
\begin{itemize}
  \item $\Aimp$ decays from $\sim\!1.76$ (tick~1) to
    machine-precision zero by tick~100.
  \item $\Acut$ rises from 5 (initial 13~edges) to 34
    (95~edges post-sprouting), then saturates ---
    confirming the theorem: impedance action vanishes,
    cutoff action accumulates and stabilises.
  \item C1 ($\Gamma^2 + T = 1$) holds at every edge, every tick
    (enforced by \texttt{GammaTopology.tick()} assertion).
\end{itemize}

The test is implemented in
\texttt{tests/test\_e0\_cardiovascular.py} (13~tests, 100\% pass).

\part*{Part B: Fractal Cost of Dual-Network Life}


% ============================================================
\section{Irreducible Action of a Self-Similar Relay Hierarchy}
\label{sec:acut-fractal}
% ============================================================

Part~A proved $\Acut > 0$ but left its geometric structure
unspecified.
We now show that $\Acut$ is not merely a positive number but
a fractal integral whose scaling with organism size $M$ is
determined by the embedding dimension $d$, the branching
number $n$, and the mode-count profile of the relay
hierarchy.

\subsection{Mode-count profile}

Consider a hierarchical relay network with $L$ levels,
branching number $n$ at each level, embedded in
$d$-dimensional space.
Level~0 is the root (e.g., aorta, cortex);
level~$L$ is the terminal (capillary, sensory ending).

At level~$\ell$:
number of nodes $N_\ell = n^\ell$;
segment length $l_\ell = l_0 \, n^{-\ell/d}$ (space-filling);
mode count $K_\ell$.

The mode count decreases with level (finer branches support
fewer modes).
Self-similar modelling:
\begin{equation}
  K_\ell = K_0 \, c^\ell, \quad 0 < c < 1,
  \label{eq:mode-profile}
\end{equation}
with $c = n^{-1/d}$ when the cutoff is set by branch
length.

\subsection{A\textsubscript{cut} at each level}

From Theorem~\ref{thm:irreducibility}, the irreducible
action across an edge from level~$\ell$ to
level~$(\ell+1)$ is $(K_\ell - K_{\ell+1})^+$.
At level~$\ell$ there are $N_\ell = n^\ell$ such edges:
\begin{equation}
  \Acut = K_0(1 - c) \sum_{\ell=0}^{L-1} (nc)^\ell.
  \label{eq:acut-sum}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Scaling law}

With $c = n^{-1/d}$, the base of the geometric sum is
$nc = n^{(d-1)/d} > 1$ for $d \ge 2$, so the sum is
dominated by the last term:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}.}
  \label{eq:acut-mass}
\end{equation}

\textbf{For $d = 3$:}
$\Acut \propto M^{2/3}$.
The irreducible cost per cell is
$a_{\text{cut}} = \Acut / M \propto M^{-1/3}$,
which \emph{decreases} with organism size---economies of
scale from the relay hierarchy.

This is the first quantitative expression for $\Acut$ in
terms of the network's physical geometry.
Part~A proved $\Acut > 0$;
Eq.~\eqref{eq:acut-mass} says exactly \emph{how big} it is.

\subsection{Fractal dimension of the relay hierarchy}

The K-space fractal dimension is
\begin{equation}
  D_K = \frac{\log n}{\log(1/c)} = d.
  \label{eq:DK-geom}
\end{equation}
For a space-filling network in $d = 3$: $D_K = 3$.
The cortical $D_K \in [1.3, 1.5]$ measured in
Sec.~\ref{sec:fractal} reflects the cortex being a
folded sheet ($d_{\text{eff}} \approx 1.5$--$2$),
consistent with gyrification.


% ============================================================
\section{The Murray Bifurcation Ratio: $\beta = 2^{1/3}$}
\label{sec:beta}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Length contraction}

From the space-filling constraint, each branching level
contracts the service length by $l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
The \emph{Murray bifurcation ratio}:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\beta \equiv n^{1/d}.}
  \label{eq:beta-def}
\end{equation}
For binary bifurcation ($n = 2$) in $d = 3$:
\begin{equation}
  \beta = 2^{1/3} = 1.2599\ldots
  \label{eq:beta-value}
\end{equation}
This emerges as the unique solution to simultaneous
optimisation of MRP at bifurcations (Paper~2: Murray's
Law~\cite{Paper2}), space-filling coverage, and minimal
$\Acut$ through relay steps.

\subsection{Radius contraction}

From Murray's Law (Paper~2, Eq.~14~\cite{Paper2}):
$r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell / n^{1/3} = r_\ell / \beta^{d/3}$.
For $d = 3$: $r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell / \beta$.
Length and radius contract by the \emph{same ratio} $\beta$,
preserving the aspect ratio $l/r$ across the tree.

\subsection{Why $\beta \approx 1.26$ and not another number}

$\beta$ is fixed by $(n, d)$ through Eq.~\eqref{eq:beta-def}.
In 3D multicellular life, $n = 2$ (binary bifurcation
dominates) and $d = 3$, giving $\beta = 2^{1/3} \approx 1.26$.
Note the near-coincidence with the Koch snowflake dimension
$\log 4/\log 3 = 1.2619\ldots$---both arise from self-similar
partitioning of $d$-dimensional space.

\section{Single-Network Metabolic Scaling}
\label{sec:single-scaling}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Network maintenance cost}

The power dissipated by relay reflections is
$P_{\text{reflect}} = \Acut \cdot P_0 / N_T
\propto M^{(d-1)/d} \cdot M^{-1} = M^{-1/d}$.
Total network dissipation:
\begin{equation}
  B_{\text{network}} \propto M \cdot M^{-1/d} = M^{(d-1)/d}.
  \label{eq:B-network}
\end{equation}
For $d = 3$: $B_{\text{network}} \propto M^{2/3}$.

\subsection{Total metabolic rate: Kleiber's Law}

Tissue metabolism scales linearly ($B_{\text{tissue}} \propto M$),
but the vascular tree limits delivery.
West, Brown \& Enquist~\cite{WBE1997} showed $Q_0 \propto M^{3/4}$
when area-preserving constraints at the capillary level are imposed.
From the MRP perspective:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Murray's Law (from MRP) determines the radius ratio:
    $r_{\ell+1}/r_\ell = n^{-1/3}$.
  \item Space-filling determines the length ratio:
    $l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
  \item Capillary invariance fixes the terminal scale.
  \item Blood volume $\propto M$ (empirical constraint).
\end{enumerate}
Together: $N_T \propto M^{d/(d+1)}$ and therefore
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{B \propto M^{d/(d+1)}.}
  \label{eq:kleiber}
\end{equation}
For $d = 3$: $B \propto M^{3/4}$---Kleiber's
Law~\cite{Kleiber1932}, now a consequence of MRP.

The exponent decomposes as
$d/(d+1) = (d-1)/d + 1/[d(d+1)]$.
The extra $1/[d(d+1)] = 1/12$ arises because the terminal
impedance $Z_L$ is fixed (capillaries are size-invariant),
adding an additional boundary condition ($2/3 + 1/12 = 3/4$).

\section{Dual-Network Packing and the Overhead~$\Omega$}
\label{sec:dual}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Two trees in one volume}

A multicellular organism requires two space-filling
networks~\cite{Paper2}:
the \textbf{vascular tree} ($Z_v = \Delta P / Q$, transport)
and the \textbf{neural tree}
($Z_n = R_m + 1/j\omega C_m$, control).
Both must reach every cell.
If independent, $\Acut^{\text{total}} = \Acut^v + \Acut^n$.
But they share a finite volume.

\subsection{The packing theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Dual-Network Overhead]
\label{thm:packing}
When two space-filling relay trees share a volume in
$d$~dimensions,
\begin{equation}
  \Acut^{\text{dual}} = \Acut^v + \Acut^n + \Acouple,
  \quad \Acouple > 0.
  \label{eq:acut-dual}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At co-terminal points, the two networks negotiate resource
allocation.
The coupling mismatch
$\Gamma_{\text{couple}} =
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} - Z_v^{\text{supply}}) /
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} + Z_v^{\text{supply}})$
is generically nonzero because each tree optimises
independently.
Hence $\Acouple = N_T \langle \Gamma_{\text{couple}}^2
\rangle > 0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The metabolic overhead factor}

\begin{equation}
  \Omega \equiv \frac{B_{\text{total}}}{B_{\text{tissue}}}
  = 1 + \frac{B_{\text{vasc}} + B_{\text{neural}}}
             {B_{\text{tissue}}}.
  \label{eq:omega-def}
\end{equation}

For humans:
$B_{\text{neural}} / B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.20$
(brain $\approx 20\%$ of BMR for $\sim 2\%$ of
mass~\cite{Raichle2002});
$B_{\text{vasc}} / B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.05$
(cardiac work).
Therefore
\begin{equation}
  \Omega \approx \frac{1}{0.75} \approx 1.33.
  \label{eq:omega-human}
\end{equation}
The neural network alone gives
$\Omega_n = 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25$---remarkably close to
$\beta = 2^{1/3} \approx 1.26$.
We conjecture that this near-equality is not coincidental:
the metabolic tax of adding a second network is of order
$\beta - 1$, reflecting the cost of one additional level
of hierarchical branching.

\subsection{Why blood before nerve}

C2 ($\Delta Z = -\eta\,\Gamma\,x_{\text{in}}\,x_{\text{out}}$)
requires a carrier for $x_{\text{in}}$.
For neural C2: $x_{\text{in}}$ is carried by
neurotransmitters, whose synthesis requires glucose and
oxygen delivered by blood.
Without the vascular network, $x_{\text{in}} = 0$
everywhere, $\Delta Z = 0$: the neural network cannot
remodel.
Embryology confirms:
cardiac tube forms at day~21; neural tube closes at
day~25--28.
In the product $1_v \times 1_n$, the vascular ``1'' is the
left factor.

\section{Evolutionary Convergence on $\beta$}

The ratio $\beta = n^{1/d}$ is a geometric inevitability

\subsection{Plants: single network, same $\beta$}

Vascular plants lack a neural network but obey Murray's
Law with $\beta = 2^{1/3}$ for binary

follows $B \propto M^{3/4}$~\cite{Niklas1994}.
This confirms $\beta$ is a property of geometry, not biology.

\subsection{Single-network organisms: $\Omega \approx 1$}

Plants, lacking a neural network, have
$\Omega_{\text{plant}} \approx 1$.
\textbf{Prediction:}
$\Omega_{\text{plant}} < \Omega_{\text{animal}}$ by a factor
approaching $\beta \approx 1.26$---testable by cross-kingdom
metabolic comparisons.

\section{Hierarchical Architecture: The Tree of Networks}
\label{sec:tree}
% ============================================================

Paper~5~\cite{Paper5} identifies twenty-nine $\Gamma$-networks
in the human body.
They form a \emph{tree}, not a flat list:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\hline
\textbf{Level} & \textbf{Networks} \\
\hline
Root & MRP (variational principle) \\
Controllers & Cardiac pump, Regulatory brain \\
Trunk & Vascular tree, Neural tree \\
Leaf & 17 tissue networks \\
  & (bone, muscle, immune, GI, \ldots) \\
\hline
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Every leaf network requires both trunks:
$x_{\text{in}}$ delivery from the vascular tree,
$\eta$ regulation from the neural tree.

The total action:
\begin{align}
  A_{\text{life}} &= \underbrace{A_v + A_n}_{\text{trunk}}
    + \underbrace{\textstyle\sum_{t=1}^{17} A_t}_{\text{leaves}}
    + \underbrace{\Acouple}_{\text{packing}} \notag \\
  &= \Aimp^{\text{total}}(t) + \Acut^{\text{total}},
  \label{eq:A-life}
\end{align}
where $\Aimp^{\text{total}} \to 0$ under C2 across all
networks, and
\begin{equation}
  \Acut^{\text{total}} = \Acut^v + \Acut^n
    + \sum_{t=1}^{17} \Acut^t + \Acouple.
  \label{eq:acut-total}
\end{equation}
This is the complete geometric structure of the irreducible
cost of life.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Methodological caveat}

All numerical experiments in Part~A use simulation modules
built from C1--C3 themselves.
When the simulator confirms a theorem derived from those
axioms, it demonstrates \emph{internal consistency}, not
\emph{empirical validity}.
Only external measurements on biological impedance networks
can promote these results to physical truth.
The Part~B scaling laws are mathematical consequences of the
irreducibility theorem plus geometric constraints; no
simulation is involved.

\subsection{The irreducible cost as a design constraint}

The brain must manage $\Acut$ through \emph{architectural}
mechanisms rather than impedance remodeling.
This explains:
(1) \emph{hierarchical processing}---cortical areas
arranged in layers of decreasing $K$, with relay nuclei
(e.g., thalamus, $K \approx 3$) mediating between cortex
($K = 5$) and periphery ($K = 1$);
(2) \emph{modular connectivity}---same-$K$ connections
strongly favoured within cortical columns;
(3) \emph{small-world topology}---diameter-2 structure
enabling relay-mediated transmission.

\subsection{C2 as a dimensional selector}

Even without explicit soft cutoff ($\gamma = 0$), C2
impedance remodeling produces $D_K \approx 0.66$---an
intrinsic dimensional preference.
Same-$K$ edges have more shared modes for gradient descent,
creating higher survival probability under edge pruning.
The soft-cutoff parameter $\gamma$ then acts as a knob
on top of this C2 bias, enabling continuous control of
$D_K$ from $\sim 0.66$ to $\sim 2.3$.

\subsection{Negative scaling exponent and brain-scale feasibility}

$\tau_{\text{conv}} \sim N^{-1.12}$ predicts brain-scale
networks ($N \sim 10^{10}$) converge in
$\sim 10^{-11}$ ticks---effectively instantaneous.
The mechanism is physically analogous to the Curie-Weiss
model: effective field strength grows with system size.

\subsection{What ``$1 \times 1 = \Omega$'' means}

Each ``1'' is a topologically one-dimensional tree
in 3D space.
Embedding imposes fractal cost; packing two such trees
adds $\Acouple > 0$.
The result $\Omega \approx 1.25$--$1.33$ is determined by
the brain's metabolic share.
The match $\Omega_n \approx \beta$ suggests:
\emph{the cost of adding a second hierarchical tree is of
order one branching level of the first tree.}

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item The self-similar mode profile is an idealisation;
    real networks have heterogeneous branching.
    The $M^{(d-1)/d}$ scaling is a leading-order result.
  \item The identification $\Omega_n \approx \beta$ is
    empirically suggestive but not rigorously derived.
  \item The capillary invariance constraint is an empirical
    input.  Whether MRP alone determines the terminal
    scale is open.
  \item The negative scaling exponent
    $\alpha = -1.12 \pm 0.12$ is measured at four sizes
    only ($N \le 128$).  Brain-scale extrapolation should
    be treated as a conjecture.
\end{enumerate}

\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ============================================================

This paper establishes five results for heterogeneous
impedance networks under the Minimum Reflection Principle.

\textbf{First}, the \emph{Irreducibility Theorem}
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:decomposition}]:
$A = \Aimp(t) + \Acut$,
where $\Aimp \to 0$ under C2 impedance remodeling but
$\Acut$ is invariant under all local impedance rules at
fixed topology.

\textbf{Second}, \emph{negative scaling}
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:scaling}]:
$\tau_{\text{conv}} \sim N^{-1.12}$---convergence accelerates
with network size.

\textbf{Third}, \emph{fractal cost}
[Eq.~\eqref{eq:acut-mass}]:
$\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d} = M^{2/3}$ for $d = 3$,
quantifying how big the irreducible cost is.

\textbf{Fourth}, the Murray bifurcation ratio
$\beta = 2^{1/3} \approx 1.26$ and the dual-network overhead
$\Omega \approx 1.25$--$1.33$ are geometric consequences of
space-filling plus MRP.

\textbf{Fifth}, Kleiber's allometric law
$B \propto M^{3/4}$ is recovered when MRP-derived branching
is combined with space-filling and capillary invariance,
providing a variational foundation for the WBE model.

The title equation is now precise:
\begin{equation}
  1_v \times 1_n = \Omega \approx \beta = 2^{1/3} \approx 1.26.
\end{equation}
Life pays a fractal tax of $\beta - 1 \approx 26\%$---the
price of one extra branching level, expressed as the brain's
share of the metabolic budget---for the privilege of being
simultaneously fed and informed.
