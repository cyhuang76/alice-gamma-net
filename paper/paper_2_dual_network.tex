\begin{abstract}
We ask: given a blood vessel with impedance
$\Zv = 8\mu L/\pi r^4$ governed by vascular C2 update,
what architecture---and what metabolic scaling laws---must
emerge?

\textbf{Part~A} derives vascular architecture from the
Minimum Reflection Principle (MRP).
A blood vessel is a hydraulic transmission line.
At every bifurcation, impedance mismatch produces a
reflection $\Gv$; C1 gives $\Gv^2 + \Tv = 1$.
We derive Murray's Law ($r_p^3 = \sum r_i^3$) as the
exact minimum of the vascular action
$\Av = \sum_j[Q_j^2 Z_j + \lambda V_j]$, with 100\%
numerical agreement for $n=2,3,4$.
Vascular C2 update ($\Delta\Zv=-\eta_v\Gv\tau_{\text{wall}}\rho$)
operates on the same principle as neural C2 but with
$\eta_v \ll \eta_n$.

\textbf{Part~B} derives the dual-network organ model.
Every organ is served by two coincident networks:
vascular ($\Gv$) and neural ($\Gn$).
Organ health $H = \Tn\cdot\Tv = (1-\Gn^2)(1-\Gv^2)$
follows from multiplicative transmission.
Four failure modes are classified by which network fails.
The organ impedance hierarchy ranks ten organs by their
resting $\Gv^2$.

\textbf{Part~C} derives metabolic scaling.
A self-similar relay hierarchy with branching number~$n$
in $d$ dimensions produces an irreducible action
$\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}$ (for $d=3$: $M^{2/3}$).
The Murray bifurcation ratio $\beta = n^{1/d} = 2^{1/3}$
sets length and radius contraction across levels.
Kleiber's Law ($B \propto M^{3/4}$) follows from
capillary invariance.
Dual-network packing yields a metabolic overhead
$\Omega \approx 1.33$, with $\Omega_n \approx \beta$.
Multi-scale verification confirms the irreducibility
theorem across cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, and
29-tissue-atlas scales.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

A blood vessel is a tube carrying fluid under pressure.
Its impedance---the ratio of pressure drop to volume
flow---is a physical fact set by geometry and fluid
properties.

Paper~1~\cite{Paper1} derives neural architecture and
the mind from the Minimum Reflection Principle (MRP).
Yet every organ is governed by a \emph{second} impedance
network: the vasculature.
The pressure wave in an artery reflects at every
bifurcation, stricture, or compliance mismatch,
precisely as an electromagnetic wave reflects at a
transmission-line discontinuity~\cite{nichols2011,
womersley1955}.

This paper derives the vascular architecture from the
same MRP, then combines both networks into the
dual-network organ model, and finally shows that
metabolic scaling laws (Murray, Kleiber) are necessary
consequences of the same irreducible topology.

The derivation follows a strictly outward chain:
single vessel (Sec.~\ref{sec:framework}) $\to$
Murray's Law (Sec.~\ref{sec:murray}) $\to$
vascular C2 (Sec.~\ref{sec:vascular-C2}) $\to$
dual-network organ model (Sec.~\ref{sec:organ-model})
$\to$ fractal cost (Sec.~\ref{sec:fractal-cost}) $\to$
Kleiber's Law (Sec.~\ref{sec:murray-kleiber}).

\section{Vascular Impedance Physics}
\label{sec:framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The blood vessel as transmission line}

A cylindrical vessel of length~$L$ and radius~$r$
carrying a Newtonian fluid of viscosity~$\mu$ has
steady-state impedance given by the Poiseuille law:
\begin{equation}
  \Zv = \frac{\Delta P}{Q} = \frac{8\mu L}{\pi r^4}.
  \label{eq:poiseuille}
\end{equation}

In the pulsatile regime, Womersley theory adds
a frequency-dependent component:
\begin{equation}
  \Zv(\omega) = \frac{8\mu L}{\pi r^4}
    \cdot F(\alpha),
  \label{eq:womersley}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = r\sqrt{\omega\rho_f/\mu}$ is the
Womersley number, $F(\alpha) \to 1$ for
$\alpha \ll 1$ (Poiseuille limit) and
$F(\alpha) \to j\alpha^2/8$ for $\alpha \gg 1$
(inertial limit)~\cite{womersley1955}.

\subsection{Three physical laws in the vascular domain}
\label{sec:vascular-laws}

\textbf{C1---Energy Conservation.}
$\Gv^2 + \Tv = 1$ at every vascular junction.

\textbf{C2---Vascular Update.}
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\Zv = -\eta_v\,\Gv\,\tau_{\text{wall}}\,\rho,
  \label{eq:C2v}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\text{wall}}$ is wall shear stress
($x_{\text{in}}$), $\rho$ is material supply
($x_{\text{out}}$), and $\eta_v$ is the vascular
update rate.

\textbf{C3---Dimensional Relay.}
Blood pressure waves carry source impedance as metadata.

\subsection{Bifurcation reflection}
\label{sec:bifurcation}

At a symmetric bifurcation ($n$ identical daughters),
$\Zv^{\text{down}} = \Zv^d / n$:
\begin{equation}
  \Gv = \frac{\Zv^d/n - \Zv^p}{\Zv^d/n + \Zv^p}.
  \label{eq:bif-gamma}
\end{equation}
Pure impedance matching ($\Gv = 0$) gives
$r_d = r_p / n^{1/4}$, which \emph{disagrees} with
Murray's Law.
The resolution: MRP minimises the \emph{total vascular
action} including metabolic maintenance cost.

\section{Murray's Law from the Vascular Action Principle}
\label{sec:murray}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Vascular action functional}

\begin{equation}
  \Av = \sum_j \bigl[Q_j^2\,Z_j + \lambda\,V_j\bigr],
  \label{eq:vascular-action}
\end{equation}
where $Q_j$ is flow, $Z_j = 8\mu L_j/\pi r_j^4$
is impedance, $V_j = \pi r_j^2 L_j$ is volume, and
$\lambda$ is the metabolic cost per unit volume.

\subsection{Derivation for symmetric $n$-branching}

Daughter contribution:
\begin{equation}
  \Av^d = \frac{Q^2\,8\mu L_d}{n\,\pi\,r_d^4}
    + n\lambda\pi r_d^2 L_d.
  \label{eq:Av-daughter}
\end{equation}
Minimising $\partial\Av^d/\partial r_d = 0$:
\begin{equation}
  r_d^6 = \frac{16\,Q^2\,\mu}{n^2\,\lambda\,\pi^2},
  \qquad
  r_p^6 = \frac{16\,Q^2\,\mu}{\lambda\,\pi^2}.
  \label{eq:rd-rp-opt}
\end{equation}
Taking the ratio:

\begin{theorem}[Murray's Law from MRP]
\label{thm:murray}
For symmetric $n$-branching of a vessel with parent
radius~$r_p$, minimisation of the vascular action
$\Av = \sum_j [Q_j^2 Z_j + \lambda V_j]$ yields
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{r_d = \frac{r_p}{n^{1/3}},}
  \label{eq:murray}
\end{equation}
equivalently $r_p^3 = \sum_{i=1}^n r_{d,i}^3$
(the cube law).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Setting $\partial\Av^d/\partial r_d = 0$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Av-daughter} gives
$r_d^6 = 16 Q^2 \mu / (n^2 \lambda \pi^2)$ and
$r_p^6 = 16 Q^2 \mu / (\lambda \pi^2)$.
The ratio $r_d^6/r_p^6 = 1/n^2$ gives
$r_d/r_p = n^{-1/3}$.
The $1/3$ exponent---rather than $1/4$ from pure
impedance matching---arises from the balance of
dissipation ($\propto r^{-4}$) and maintenance
($\propto r^{+2}$).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Physics, not optimisation]
The $1/3$ exponent arises from the balance of dissipation
($\propto r^{-4}$) and maintenance ($\propto r^{+2}$).
Pure impedance matching gives $1/4$; MRP gives $1/3$.
Murray's Law is a physical law of impedance networks.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The Windkessel model: arterial compliance
  as a capacitor}
\label{sec:windkessel}

The aorta and large arteries are not rigid pipes:
their elastic walls store energy during systole and
release it during diastole, smoothing pulsatile flow.
In the transmission-line analogy, wall compliance is
a \emph{capacitor} $C_w = \Delta V / \Delta P$
(volume change per unit pressure).

The simplest Windkessel model treats the arterial
tree as an $RC$ circuit:
\begin{equation}
  Z_{\text{WK}} = R + \frac{1}{j\omega C_w}\,,
  \label{eq:windkessel}
\end{equation}
where $R$ is the peripheral resistance
($\approx 8\mu L / \pi r^4$ from Poiseuille) and
$C_w$ is the total arterial compliance
($\approx 1$--$2$\,mL/mmHg in a healthy young adult;
Westerhof et~al., 2009).

\begin{remark}[Ageing as capacitor degradation]
\label{rem:windkessel-ageing}
With age, collagen cross-linking and elastin
fragmentation reduce $C_w$ monotonically
(cf.\ the Second Law of $\Gamma$-Net, Paper~1).
The consequences are purely electrical:
\begin{itemize}
  \item $C_w \downarrow \;\Rightarrow\;$
    systolic pressure rises (capacitor stores less),
  \item diastolic pressure falls
    (less stored energy to release),
  \item pulse pressure $\Delta P$ widens,
  \item reflected waves arrive during systole
    rather than diastole, augmenting peak pressure.
\end{itemize}
All four are the canonical haemodynamic changes of
ageing.  In $\Gamma$-language, $C_w \downarrow$
increases wall $Z$, increasing $\Gamma^2$ at every
bifurcation---exactly the progressive
$\Gamma^2_{\text{basal}} \uparrow$ predicted by the
Second Law.
The clinical marker PWV $= \sqrt{1/(\rho C_w)}$
therefore rises with $C_w^{-1/2}$, providing a
non-invasive proxy for vascular $\Gamma^2$.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Why two networks: multiplicative coupling}

Neural C2 requires material substrate ($\rho$) from
vascular delivery.
Vascular C2 requires neural control (autonomic signals
regulate vascular tone).
The two networks are \emph{mutually prerequisite},
and their coupling is \textbf{multiplicative, not
additive}:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\text{Dual network}
  = \underbrace{\text{Vascular}}_{\text{supplies } P_{\text{in}}}
  \;\otimes\;
  \underbrace{\text{Neural}}_{\text{uses } P_{\text{in}}}\,,}
  \label{eq:tensor-dual}
\end{equation}
where $\otimes$ denotes tensor product.
The effective SNR of the combined system is:
\[
  \text{SNR} = \frac{P_{\text{in}}
  \cdot (1 - \Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}})
  \cdot \eta \cdot \Gamma^2_{\text{signal}}}
  {P_{\text{noise}}}\,.
\]
If \emph{any} factor is zero, the product is zero.
No amount of neural excellence compensates for
$P_{\text{in}} = 0$; no amount of vascular health
compensates for $\eta = 0$.
This is the physics of why a stroke (vascular) and
anaesthesia (neural) produce the \emph{same} outcome:
consciousness off.

\paragraph{The 200\,$\mu$m diffusion limit.}
Oxygen diffusion in tissue reaches
$\sim$200\,$\mu$m before concentration drops below
the metabolic threshold.
Once an embryo exceeds this radius ($\sim$64 cells),
peripheral cells have $P_{\text{in}} = 0$;
all multiplicative factors vanish and differentiation
halts.
A transport system (heart + vessels) must exist
\emph{before} the embryo can grow beyond 200\,$\mu$m.
This is a C1 theorem, not an evolutionary contingency:
\[
  \forall\, t:\quad P_{\text{in}}(t) > 0 \;\implies\;
  \text{cardiovascular system precedes all others.}
\]

\paragraph{Nested C1: mother and foetus.}
The foetus operates inside a two-layer C1 structure:
\begin{align}
  \text{Layer 1 (maternal):} &\quad
    P_{\text{in}}^{\text{fetal}}
    = P_{\text{in}}^{\text{maternal}}
    \cdot (1 - \Gamma^2_{\text{placenta}})\,, \nonumber\\
  \text{Layer 2 (foetal):} &\quad
    P_{\text{in}}^{\text{organ}}
    = P_{\text{in}}^{\text{fetal}}
    \cdot (1 - \Gamma^2_{\text{vessel},i})\,.
    \label{eq:nested-C1}
\end{align}
The placental $\Gamma^2$ determines the foetal energy
budget.
Maternal stress (cortisol $\to$ vasoconstriction
$\to \Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}} \uparrow$ on the
maternal side) directly reduces foetal $P_{\text{in}}$,
providing a first-principles explanation for the
well-documented effect of prenatal stress on
neurodevelopment
(Remark~\ref{rem:social-referencing}).

\begin{remark}[Neurovascular coupling: the vascular
  network sets the neural SNR ceiling]
\label{rem:neurovascular-coupling}
The mutual dependence of P1 and P2 has a precise
quantitative form.
The input power available to a neuron is:
\begin{equation}
  P_{\text{in}}(t) = P_{\text{basal}} \cdot
  \bigl(1 - \Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}}(t)\bigr)\,,
  \label{eq:Pin-vessel}
\end{equation}
where $\Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}}$ is the vascular
reflection coefficient at the local capillary bed.
Since $\theta = P_{\text{noise}} / P_{\text{in}}$
(Paper~1), a rise in $\Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}}$
lowers $P_{\text{in}}$ and thereby degrades neural
SNR \emph{regardless of the neural network's own
state}.

The coupling loop is mediated by astrocytes:
\[
  \text{neuron } \Gamma^2 \uparrow
  \;\xrightarrow{\text{astrocyte}}
  \text{NO, prostaglandin}
  \;\xrightarrow{\text{vessel}}
  Z_v \downarrow
  \;\Rightarrow\;
  Q \uparrow
  \;\Rightarrow\;
  P_{\text{in}} \; \text{restored.}
\]
This is \emph{neurovascular coupling} (NVC);
the BOLD signal measured by fMRI is its
haemodynamic trace.

Three clinical consequences follow directly:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Vascular disease $\to$ emotional
    disorders}: atherosclerosis raises
    $\Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}}$ chronically,
    reducing $P_{\text{in}}$, degrading SNR,
    and producing the ``vascular depression''
    phenotype (Alexopoulos et~al., 1997).
  \item \textbf{Exercise $\to$ emotional improvement}:
    exercise lowers $\Gamma^2_{\text{vessel}}$
    via C2 vascular remodelling
    (Remark~\ref{rem:exercise}), raising
    $P_{\text{in}}$ and restoring neural SNR.
  \item \textbf{Diabetes $\to$ cognitive decline}:
    fluctuating blood glucose causes intermittent
    $P_{\text{in}}$ drops; C2 updates stall during each
    trough, shrinking the effective
    $\mathcal{S}_{\text{eff}}$ maintenance window
    and accelerating cognitive ageing.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Organ health}

At each organ, the neural network transmits $\Tn = 1-\Gn^2$
and the vascular network transmits $\Tv = 1-\Gv^2$.
Series connection:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{H = \Tn \cdot \Tv = (1-\Gn^2)(1-\Gv^2).}
  \label{eq:dual-health}
\end{equation}
$H=1$: perfect transmission; $H=0$: at least one
network totally blocked.

\subsection{Thermal bus}

The vascular network carries heat:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{Q}_{\text{organ}} = c_p\,\rho_f\,Q
    \,(T_{\text{art}} - T_{\text{ven}}).
  \label{eq:thermal-bus}
\end{equation}
When $\Gv^2$ increases, $Q$ decreases and thermal
exchange is compromised.

\begin{remark}[Blood as the actively manufactured
  transport bus]
\label{rem:blood-bus}
Blood is not a passive residue of tissue metabolism
but a continuously manufactured transport substrate:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Cellular fraction}: red blood cells,
    white blood cells, and platelets are produced by
    haematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow
    (C2 network \#15, Table~5 of Paper~3)
    and recycled by the spleen.
  \item \textbf{Plasma proteins}: albumin, fibrinogen,
    and complement factors are synthesised by the liver
    (C2 network \#12) and maintain osmotic pressure,
    coagulation, and immune function.
  \item \textbf{Dual cargo}: the same flow simultaneously
    delivers fresh material
    ($\rho \equiv x_{\text{out}}$ for downstream C2)
    and removes metabolic waste (lactate, urea, CO$_2$).
\end{itemize}
In the $\Gamma$-framework, blood is the
\emph{trunk bus} whose impedance
$Z_v = 8\mu L/\pi r^4$ determines C2 supply to every
organ.
When $Z_v$ rises (atherosclerosis, dehydration,
anaemia), \emph{all} downstream
$x_{\text{out},i}$ decrease, stalling C2 globally.
The vascular bus is therefore the universal
$\rho$-bottleneck of the dual-network model.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Emotion is a vascular event observed
  by the brain]
\label{rem:emotion-vascular}
In the $\Gamma$-framework, an emotion has three
physical phases:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Detection (P1, neural):}
    the amygdala detects $\Gamma^2 \neq 0$
    ($\sim$30\,ms; Paper~1).
  \item \textbf{Execution (P2, vascular):}
    sympathetic or parasympathetic C3 relay
    modulates vascular $Z_v$---producing the
    physical sensations universally reported as
    ``feeling an emotion'': flushing (anger),
    pallor (fear), warmth (joy),
    chest heaviness (grief).
  \item \textbf{Perception (P2$\to$P1):}
    interoceptive afferents from the vascular
    periphery feed back to the insular cortex,
    which labels the bodily state as an emotion.
\end{enumerate}
This is consistent with the James--Lange theory
(1884): the subjective experience of emotion
\emph{is} the brain's perception of its own
vascular response, not the cause of it.
The $\Gamma$-framework adds precision: the
``feeling'' is the insular cortex reading the
$\Gamma^2$ change in the vascular tree that was
triggered by the amygdala's neural $\Gamma^2$ signal.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Vasovagal syncope as a P2 safety valve]
\label{rem:vasovagal}
When emotional $\Gamma^2$ exceeds a threshold (extreme
joy, grief, or pain), the vagus nerve fires a
paradoxical parasympathetic response:
heart rate drops (bradycardia) and peripheral vessels
dilate simultaneously.
Blood pressure collapses, cerebral $P_{\text{in}}$
falls below the consciousness threshold~$\theta$
(Paper~1, Theorem~4), and the subject faints.

This is a \emph{protective circuit breaker}:
rather than allowing sustained high-$\Gamma^2$
signalling to overheat the neural network,
the vascular system shuts down cerebral perfusion,
forcing consciousness offline.
Recovery occurs automatically when the horizontal
posture restores venous return and $P_{\text{in}}$
rises above~$\theta$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Summer irritability as thermal-bus
  SNR degradation]
\label{rem:thermal-irritability}
The consciousness threshold
$\theta = 4 k_B T R_{\text{eff}} \Delta f / P_{\text{in}}$
(Paper~1) depends linearly on body temperature~$T$.
When ambient temperature rises, the thermal bus
(Eq.~\ref{eq:thermal-bus}) becomes less efficient:
$T_{\text{art}} - T_{\text{ven}}$ narrows, reducing
$\dot{Q}_{\text{organ}}$ and allowing core $T$
to drift upward.

Even a $+0.5$\,K rise in $T$ increases $P_{\text{noise}}$
by $\sim 0.16\%$---negligible in absolute terms, but
$\Gamma^2$ operates on margins: the amygdala's
detection threshold is already near the noise floor,
so any SNR degradation lowers the barrier for
emotional triggering.
This provides a first-principles explanation for the
well-documented summer increase in violent crime
(Anderson, 2001) and road rage:
\emph{heat does not cause anger; it reduces the
$\Gamma^2$ margin between detection and triggering}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Exercise as vascular C2 training]
\label{rem:exercise}
Aerobic exercise increases cardiac output, raising
shear stress $\tau_{\text{wall}}$ on endothelial cells.
Since vascular C2 is $\Delta Z_v = -\eta_v \Gamma_v
\tau_{\text{wall}} \rho$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:C2v-detail}),
elevated $\tau_{\text{wall}}$ directly drives
$Z_v \downarrow$: the vessel walls remodel to
reduce impedance (vasodilation, angiogenesis,
reduced stiffness).
The downstream effects cascade through the
entire framework:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item $Z_v \downarrow \;\Rightarrow\;
    \Gamma_v^2 \downarrow$ at every bifurcation;
  \item Blood flow $Q \uparrow \;\Rightarrow\;$
    thermal bus capacity $\dot{Q}_{\text{organ}}
    \uparrow$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:thermal-bus});
  \item Body temperature better regulated
    $\Rightarrow\; P_{\text{noise}} \downarrow
    \;\Rightarrow\; \theta \downarrow
    \;\Rightarrow\;$ SNR$\uparrow$;
  \item Amygdala triggering threshold rises
    $\Rightarrow\;$ emotional stability improves.
\end{enumerate}
This provides a first-principles explanation for the
well-established antidepressant effect of exercise:
it is not ``endorphins'' (a correlation) but a
\emph{direct vascular C2 improvement} that raises
the SNR of the entire neural network.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Sleep as the glymphatic C2
  maintenance window]
\label{rem:glymphatic-sleep}
During slow-wave sleep, the brain's interstitial
space expands by $\sim$60\% (Xie et~al., 2013)
and cerebrospinal fluid flow increases, flushing
metabolic waste (amyloid-$\beta$, tau, lactate)
through perivascular channels---the
\emph{glymphatic system}.

In $\Gamma$-Net terms, this is the lymphatic tree's
scheduled C2 maintenance cycle:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Wake}: neural C2 is active
    (learning), producing metabolic waste that
    accumulates as impedance debris
    ($Z_{\text{waste}} \uparrow$).
  \item \textbf{Sleep}: neural C2 is suspended;
    the glymphatic system activates, clearing
    $Z_{\text{waste}}$ and restoring vascular
    $\Gamma^2$ to baseline.
\end{itemize}
Chronic sleep deprivation prevents this maintenance,
allowing $Z_{\text{waste}}$ to accumulate
$\to \Gamma^2_{\text{basal}} \uparrow$ $\to$
accelerated ageing and neurodegeneration
(cf.\ Alzheimer's amyloid hypothesis).
Sleep is not rest---it is \emph{P2 maintenance}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Fever as Arrhenius-accelerated
  immune C2]
\label{rem:fever}
Fever raises body temperature by $+1$--$4$\,K.
By the Arrhenius equation, the rate of biochemical
reactions (including immune C2 updates)
scales as $\exp[-E_a / k_B T]$.
A $+2$\,K increase from $37\,^{\circ}$C to
$39\,^{\circ}$C accelerates immune reaction rates
by $\sim$20--40\% (depending on activation energy).

In $\Gamma$-Net terms, fever is a
\emph{controlled C1 violation} that trades
short-term thermal stress for accelerated C2:
\[
  \eta_{\text{immune}}(T) \propto
  \exp\!\bigl[-E_a / k_B T\bigr]
  \;\xrightarrow{T \uparrow}\;
  \eta_{\text{immune}} \uparrow\,.
\]
The cost: $P_{\text{noise}}$ rises (SNR degrades),
producing the cognitive fog and irritability of
fever.
The benefit: faster pathogen clearance.
Antipyretic drugs that suppress fever remove the
$\eta$ boost, potentially prolonging infection---a
prediction consistent with clinical observations
(Brandts et~al., 1997).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Four irreducible tissue types}
\label{sec:four-tissues}

C2 has four prerequisites:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Signal} ($x_{\text{in}}$) to detect
    mismatch $\Gamma$ $\to$ \textbf{Nervous tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Material substrate} ($x_{\text{out}}$)
    delivered by pipe network $\to$
    \textbf{Connective (vascular) tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Mechanical actuator} to apply force
    for structural update $\to$ \textbf{Muscle tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Boundary} defining spatial domain of $Z$
    measurement $\to$ \textbf{Epithelial tissue}.
\end{enumerate}
The dependency cycle is closed:
\[
  \text{Nervous} \xrightarrow{\text{needs }\rho}
  \text{Connective} \xrightarrow{\text{needs pump}}
  \text{Muscle} \xrightarrow{\text{needs boundary}}
  \text{Epithelial} \xrightarrow{\text{needs control}}
  \text{Nervous}.
\]
Removing any one tissue collapses C2.
\textbf{Four is both necessary and sufficient for C2
viability.}

\section{Irreducible Action of a Self-Similar Relay Hierarchy}
\label{sec:fractal-cost}
% ============================================================

Paper~1~\cite{Paper1} proved $\Acut > 0$ (the
Dimensional Cost Irreducibility Theorem) but left its
geometric structure unspecified.
This section asks: \emph{how big} is $\Acut$?

\subsection{Mode-count profile}

Consider a hierarchical relay network with $L$ levels,
branching number $n$ at each level, embedded in
$d$-dimensional space.
Level~0 is the root (aorta, cortex); level~$L$ the
terminal (capillary, sensory ending).
At level~$\ell$: $N_\ell = n^\ell$ nodes,
$l_\ell = l_0\,n^{-\ell/d}$ (space-filling), and
mode count:
\begin{equation}
  K_\ell = K_0\,c^\ell, \quad 0 < c < 1,
  \label{eq:mode-profile}
\end{equation}
with $c = n^{-1/d}$ when the cutoff is set by branch
length.

\subsection{$\Acut$ at each level}

From the Irreducibility Theorem (Paper~1,
Theorem~1~\cite{Paper1}), at level~$\ell$ there are
$N_\ell = n^\ell$ edges, each contributing
$(K_\ell - K_{\ell+1})^+$:
\begin{equation}
  \Acut = K_0(1 - c) \sum_{\ell=0}^{L-1} (nc)^\ell.
  \label{eq:acut-sum}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Scaling law}

With $c = n^{-1/d}$, the base $nc = n^{(d-1)/d} > 1$
for $d \ge 2$, so the sum is dominated by the last
term:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}.}
  \label{eq:acut-mass}
\end{equation}
\textbf{For $d = 3$}: $\Acut \propto M^{2/3}$.
The per-cell cost $a_{\text{cut}} = \Acut/M \propto
M^{-1/3}$ \emph{decreases} with organism size---economies
of scale from the relay hierarchy.

\section{Murray's Ratio and Metabolic Scaling}
\label{sec:murray-kleiber}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The Murray bifurcation ratio}

Each branching level contracts the service length by
$l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
The bifurcation ratio:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\beta \equiv n^{1/d}.}
  \label{eq:beta-def}
\end{equation}
For binary bifurcation ($n=2$) in $d=3$:
$\beta = 2^{1/3} = 1.2599\ldots$
From Murray's Law (Eq.~\ref{eq:murray}):
$r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell/n^{1/3}$; for $d=3$,
$r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell/\beta$.

\subsection{Network maintenance cost}

Relay reflection power:
$P_{\text{reflect}} = \Acut \cdot P_0/N_T
\propto M^{(d-1)/d} \cdot M^{-1} = M^{-1/d}$.
Total network dissipation:
\begin{equation}
  B_{\text{network}} \propto M \cdot M^{-1/d}
  = M^{(d-1)/d}.
  \label{eq:B-network}
\end{equation}
For $d=3$: $B_{\text{network}} \propto M^{2/3}$.

\begin{theorem}[Kleiber's Law from MRP]
\label{thm:kleiber}
For a self-similar vascular tree with branching number~$n$
in $d$ spatial dimensions, MRP implies
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{B \propto M^{d/(d+1)}.}
  \label{eq:kleiber}
\end{equation}
For $d=3$: $B \propto M^{3/4}$---Kleiber's
Law~\cite{Kleiber1932}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Murray's Law (Theorem~\ref{thm:murray}) gives
$r_{\ell+1}/r_\ell = n^{-1/3}$.
Space-filling determines the length ratio
$l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
Capillary invariance fixes the terminal scale;
blood volume $\propto M$.
Combining: the number of terminal units is
$N_T \propto M^{d/(d+1)}$, and since each terminal
unit has a fixed metabolic rate, $B \propto N_T
\propto M^{d/(d+1)}$.
\end{proof}

  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{../figures/fig_p2_kleiber_scatter.pdf}
  \caption{Kleiber's Law verification.

\subsection{Volumetric heat density}

Every active interface dissipates $\Gamma_i^2 P_{\text{in},i}$
as heat (Paper~0, Corollary~1~\cite{Paper0}).
A tissue with $K$ modes:
\begin{equation}
  q \propto K \cdot \avg{\Gamma^2} \cdot P_{\text{in}}
  / V_{\text{node}}.
  \label{eq:vol-heat}
\end{equation}
The human brain ($K=5$) is $\sim$2\% of body mass but
$\sim$25\% of basal metabolic heat---because its
per-node mode count is largest.

\section{Dual-Network Packing}
\label{sec:dual-packing}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Two trees in one volume}

A multicellular organism requires two space-filling
networks: the \textbf{vascular tree}
($Z_v = \Delta P/Q$, transport) and the
\textbf{neural tree}
($Z_n = R_m + 1/j\omega C_m$, control).

\begin{theorem}[Dual-Network Overhead]
\label{thm:packing}
When two space-filling relay trees share a volume in
$d$~dimensions,
\begin{equation}
  \Acut^{\text{dual}} = \Acut^v + \Acut^n + \Acouple,
  \quad \Acouple > 0.
  \label{eq:acut-dual}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At co-terminal points, the two networks negotiate
resource allocation.
The coupling mismatch
$\Gamma_{\text{couple}} =
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} - Z_v^{\text{supply}}) /
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} + Z_v^{\text{supply}})$
is generically nonzero because each tree optimises
independently.
Hence $\Acouple = N_T \avg{\Gamma_{\text{couple}}^2} > 0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The metabolic overhead factor}

\begin{equation}
  \Omega \equiv \frac{B_{\text{total}}}{B_{\text{tissue}}}
  = 1 + \frac{B_{\text{vasc}} + B_{\text{neural}}}
             {B_{\text{tissue}}}.
  \label{eq:omega-def}
\end{equation}
For humans:
$B_{\text{neural}}/B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.20$;
$B_{\text{vasc}}/B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.05$.
Therefore $\Omega \approx 1/0.75 \approx 1.33$.
The neural network alone gives
$\Omega_n = 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25 \approx \beta = 2^{1/3}$.

\subsection{Why blood before nerve}

C2 requires a carrier for $x_{\text{in}}$:
neurotransmitter synthesis needs glucose and oxygen from
blood.
Without the vascular network, $x_{\text{in}} = 0$,
$\Delta Z = 0$: the neural network cannot remodel.
Embryology confirms: cardiac tube at day~21; neural tube
closure at day~25--28.
In the product $1_v \times 1_n$, the vascular ``1'' is
the left factor.

The deeper reason is C1 applied to the
mother--fetus system.
The embryo develops inside the mother's body
at $T \approx 37\,^{\circ}$C.
Every metabolising cell produces heat
($\dot{Q} = \Gamma^2 P_{\text{in}}$).
Without a thermal bus to export this heat to the
mother's vascular tree, the embryo's temperature
would rise uncontrollably---violating C1
(energy has no outflow path).
The \emph{placenta} is the C3 impedance-matching
relay between the maternal and foetal vascular trees:
\[
  Z_{\text{maternal}} \;\underset{\text{placenta}}
    {\longleftrightarrow}\; Z_{\text{foetal}}.
\]
Placental matching ensures $\Gamma^2_{\text{mat-foet}}
\approx 0$, maximising heat and nutrient transfer.
The developmental sequence is therefore not a design
choice but a physical necessity:

\begin{tabular}{lll}
\toprule
Day & Event & $\Gamma$-Net reason \\
\midrule
21 & Cardiac tube (first heartbeat) &
  C1: thermal bus must exist first \\
25--28 & Neural tube closure &
  C2: control network requires $\rho$ from P2 \\
$5+$ wk & Organ differentiation &
  C2 targets: need both P1 and P2 in place \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\begin{remark}[The fontanelle as a C1 safety valve]
\label{rem:fontanelle}
The neonatal skull contains open sutures
(fontanelles) that do not fuse until 12--18 months.
This is commonly attributed to birth-canal passage,
but the $\Gamma$-framework reveals a deeper C1 reason.

During postnatal brain growth the neural network
(P1) expands rapidly, increasing metabolic heat
production.
The vascular thermal bus (P2) must scale in
parallel: new capillaries must invade expanding
cortex.
Open fontanelles permit the cranial volume to
increase, allowing the P2 thermal bus to grow
alongside P1.
If the sutures fuse prematurely
(\emph{craniosynostosis}):
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Cranial volume is locked;
  \item Intracranial pressure rises,
    compressing blood vessels:
    $Z_v \uparrow \;\Rightarrow\; Q \downarrow$;
  \item Thermal bus capacity is capped while
    neural metabolism continues to rise;
  \item C1 is violated: $\dot{Q}_{\text{in}}
    > \dot{Q}_{\text{out}}$ $\Rightarrow$
    local temperature rise $\Rightarrow$
    neuronal damage.
\end{enumerate}
Untreated craniosynostosis leads to brain injury and
death---a direct C1 prediction.

Three related neonatal observations confirm the
same principle:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ll}
\toprule
Observation & C1 interpretation \\
\midrule
Amniotic fluid &
  Maternal thermal bus extension (liquid cooling) \\
Placenta &
  Mother--foetus C3 relay ($Z$-matching) \\
Fontanelle &
  P2 thermal bus expansion window \\
Hydrocephalus &
  Fluid compresses vessels $=$ thermal bus failure \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
All four are instances of the same physical
constraint: C1 requires that metabolic heat
production never exceed thermal bus capacity.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Thermoregulation as the developmental
  ignition signal]
\label{rem:thermoregulation-trigger}
A newborn's ``start-up sequence'' recapitulates
the C1 priority chain:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item First breath $\to$ pulmonary circulation
    activates $\to$ P2 begins independent operation.
  \item First feed (milk) $\to$ metabolic fuel
    supplied $\to$ thermal bus has energy to burn.
  \item Thermoregulation established $\to$ the infant
    can maintain $T \approx 37\,^{\circ}$C autonomously
    $\to$ \textbf{C1 is independently satisfied
    for the first time}.
  \item Full-body developmental growth begins.
\end{enumerate}
Step~3 is the gate: until the thermal bus is
self-sustaining, the body cannot increase metabolic
load (growing organs) without violating C1.
This is why premature infants are placed in
incubators---the incubator is an \emph{artificial
thermal bus} substituting for the mother's P2,
and the clinical criterion for discharge includes
the ability to maintain body temperature
independently.
\end{remark}

When C1 is marginally violated (e.g.\ perinatal

\emph{the highest-$K$ organ is always the first to

\subsection{The tri-network body: arterial, venous,
  and lymphatic trees}
\label{sec:tri-network}

The preceding analysis treats the vasculature as a
single tree, but the body contains \emph{three}
distinct fluid-transport networks, each a C3 relay
hierarchy:

\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
Network & Direction & Cargo & $\Gamma$-role \\
\midrule
Arterial tree &
  Heart $\to$ periphery &
  O$_2$, glucose, hormones &
  Energy delivery C3 relay \\
Venous tree &
  Periphery $\to$ heart &
  CO$_2$, lactate, urea &
  Waste return C3 relay \\
Lymphatic tree &
  Interstitium $\to$ veins &
  Immune cells, excess fluid &
  Immune + clearance C3 relay \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Each tree is a $K$-staircase:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Arterial}: aorta ($Z \approx 6 \times 10^6$\,
    Pa\,s\,m$^{-3}$) $\to$ large artery $\to$ small artery
    $\to$ arteriole $\to$ capillary
    ($Z \approx 10^{13}$\,Pa\,s\,m$^{-3}$).
    The $\sim 10^7$-fold impedance jump is distributed
    across $\sim$15 branching generations
    (Murray, 1926), each reducing $\Gamma^2$ per stage.
  \item \textbf{Venous}: capillary $\to$ venule $\to$
    small vein $\to$ large vein $\to$ vena cava.
    This tree mirrors the arterial staircase in reverse,
    but with lower wall stiffness (larger compliance),
    acting as a volume reservoir.
  \item \textbf{Lymphatic}: initial lymphatics
    (blind-ended, $K=1$) $\to$
    collecting vessels (with valves, $K=2$) $\to$
    lymph nodes (immune relay, $K=3$) $\to$
    thoracic duct $\to$ subclavian vein.
    Lymph nodes are C3 relay stations where
    $K$-dimension increases: antigen presentation
    converts a simple molecular signal ($K=1$) into
    an adaptive immune response ($K \geq 3$).
\end{itemize}

The organ health index generalises to:
\begin{equation}
  H = T_n \cdot T_{v,\text{art}} \cdot
      T_{v,\text{ven}} \cdot T_{\ell}\,,
  \label{eq:tri-health}
\end{equation}
where $T_{\ell} = 1 - \Gamma_{\ell}^2$ is the
lymphatic transmission.
Setting any factor to zero destroys organ viability.

\begin{remark}[Arterial stiffening as progressive
  $\Gamma^2$ increase]
\label{rem:arterial-stiffening}
Arterial stiffening (arteriosclerosis) increases wall
impedance $Z_{\text{wall}}$ while downstream tissue
impedance remains unchanged.
The result is a progressive rise in $\Gamma^2$ at
every bifurcation, detectable as increased
\emph{pulse wave velocity} (PWV).
The Moens--Korteweg equation gives
$\text{PWV} = \sqrt{E h / 2 \rho r}$,
where $E$ is the elastic modulus,
$h$ the wall thickness, $r$ the radius,
and $\rho$ the blood density.
As $E$ increases with age (collagen cross-linking,
elastin degradation), $Z_{\text{wall}}$ rises and
$\Gamma^2 = |(Z_{\text{wall}} - Z_{\text{tissue}})|^2
/ |Z_{\text{wall}} + Z_{\text{tissue}}|^2$ increases
monotonically.
The 2015 AHA/ACC guidelines recognise
PWV $> 10$\,m/s as an independent cardiovascular
risk factor (Townsend et~al., 2015)---in
$\Gamma$-language, this is a threshold on the
organ-level $\Gamma^2_{v}$.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Hypertension as baroreceptor C3
  relay miscalibration]
\label{rem:hypertension}
The arterial baroreflex is a C3 feedback loop:
baroreceptors in the carotid sinus and aortic arch
measure pulse-wave amplitude
(mechanical $\to$ neural C3 relay;
Paper~0 Remark)
and modulate sympathetic tone to maintain $Z_v$
within the matched range.
\emph{Essential hypertension} is a C2 failure
of this relay: chronic elevation of $Z_v$
causes the baroreceptor's reference $Z$-pattern
to adapt upward (C2 writes the new ``normal''),
resetting the feedback target.
Treatment by medication ($Z_v \downarrow$ via
vasodilators) or exercise ($Z_v \downarrow$ via
endothelial C2) must overcome this adapted
$\mathcal{S}_{\text{eff}}$.
This predicts that abrupt antihypertensive
withdrawal produces overshoot: the C2-adapted
reference has not yet been rewritten.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Microcirculation as the terminal
  $K$-staircase]
\label{rem:microcirculation}
The capillary bed is the terminal matching section
where arterial wave impedance meets tissue impedance.
Pre-capillary sphincters act as \emph{variable gates}
($G_i$), modulating local $\Gamma^2$ in response to
tissue metabolic demand (metabolic autoregulation).
Diabetic microangiopathy thickens the capillary
basement membrane, increasing $Z$ at the terminal
matching point and elevating $\Gamma^2_{\text{cap}}$.
This explains why diabetic complications affect
\emph{end-organs} (retina, kidney, nerves)
preferentially: these organs depend on the
terminal staircase being precisely matched.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Venous insufficiency as return-path
  $\Gamma^2$ failure]
\label{rem:venous}
The venous tree relies on valves to maintain
unidirectional flow against gravity.
Valve failure allows retrograde flow, creating a
standing wave between the damaged valve and the next
competent valve.
A standing wave is a region of high $\Gamma^2$
(persistent reflection)---the clinical presentation
is \emph{varicose veins}: dilated, tortuous vessels
with elevated venous pressure.
In the $\Gamma$-framework, varicosities are the
vascular equivalent of reverberating neural trauma
(Paper~4): a C3 relay failure that produces a
self-sustaining reflection pattern.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Lymphoedema as third-network relay
  failure]
\label{rem:lymphoedema}
The lymphatic tree's C3 relay function is to return
excess interstitial fluid and immune cells to the
venous system.
When lymphatic relay nodes are damaged (surgical
removal, radiation, filariasis), the local
$\Gamma_{\ell}^2 \to 1$: fluid cannot be transmitted
and accumulates as \emph{lymphoedema}.
This is the exact analogue of spinal cord injury
(Paper~1): severing a C3 relay produces
$\Gamma = 1$ on all downstream channels.
The prediction is that lymphoedema severity should
correlate with the number of relay nodes removed,
not with the volume of tissue resected---consistent
with the sentinel lymph node paradigm in oncologic
surgery.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The hepatic portal system: a dual-input
  C3 relay]
\label{rem:portal}
The liver receives blood from two sources
simultaneously: the hepatic artery
($\sim$25\,\% of flow, high $Z$, oxygen-rich)
and the portal vein
($\sim$75\,\% of flow, low $Z$, nutrient-rich
from the GI tract).
This is the only organ with a \emph{dual-input}
C3 relay topology.

In $\Gamma$-Net terms, the liver performs impedance
matching between two trees of different $K$:
\[
  \Gamma_{\text{liver}} =
  \frac{Z_{\text{portal}} \| Z_{\text{hepatic}}
        - Z_{\text{sinusoid}}}
       {Z_{\text{portal}} \| Z_{\text{hepatic}}
        + Z_{\text{sinusoid}}}\,,
\]
where $\|$ denotes parallel combination.
This unique topology explains three clinical facts:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Liver failure cascades globally}:
    the liver is the only relay station that processes
    both circulatory and digestive $Z$-patterns.
    Failure ($\Gamma_{\text{liver}} \to 1$) blocks
    both networks simultaneously.
  \item \textbf{Cirrhosis produces portal hypertension}:
    fibrosis increases $Z_{\text{sinusoid}}$,
    mismatching the parallel input, raising
    $\Gamma^2$ and backpressure into the portal vein.
  \item \textbf{Hepatic first-pass metabolism}:
    drugs absorbed from the GI tract encounter the
    liver's $Z$-matching before reaching systemic
    circulation---a natural impedance filter that
    attenuates high-$\Gamma$ substances.
\end{enumerate}
\end{remark}

\subsection{Joints as impedance relays}

\begin{remark}[C3 as interfacial transition relay:
  a universal principle]
\label{rem:c3-relay}
The cartilage example above is a special case of a
general physical principle: whenever two tissues of
impedance $Z_1$ and $Z_2$ ($Z_1 \neq Z_2$) share an
interface, the direct junction produces a reflection
coefficient $\Gamma = (Z_2 - Z_1)/(Z_2 + Z_1)$ that
wastes energy as heat.
Inserting an intermediate layer at
$Z_{\text{mid}} = \sqrt{Z_1 Z_2}$ (the geometric mean)
splits one large reflection into two smaller ones,
each with $|\Gamma| = |\sqrt{Z_2/Z_1} - 1|/
(\sqrt{Z_2/Z_1} + 1)$, reducing total reflected power.
This is the quarter-wave transformer condition in
RF engineering~\cite{Pozar2011}, anti-reflection coating
in optics, and impedance-grading horn in acoustics.

In biology, every major tissue--tissue interface
develops such a transition layer:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Periosteum}: bone ($Z \approx 120$)
    $\leftrightarrow$ soft tissue ($Z \approx 50$),
    bridged by a fibrovascular membrane.
  \item \textbf{Fascia}: organ parenchyma
    $\leftrightarrow$ surrounding vasculature/nerve,
    bridged by connective tissue sheaths.
  \item \textbf{Adventitia}: arterial smooth muscle
    $\leftrightarrow$ perivascular tissue,
    the outermost vessel wall layer.
  \item \textbf{Myelin sheath}: axonal cytoplasm
    ($Z_{\text{axon}}$) $\leftrightarrow$ extracellular
    fluid ($Z_{\text{ECF}}$), providing impedance
    grading that enables saltatory conduction.
  \item \textbf{Basement membrane}: epithelium
    $\leftrightarrow$ connective tissue,
    a universal boundary relay.
  \item \textbf{Cartilage}: bone $\leftrightarrow$
    synovial fluid (the case above).
\end{enumerate}

At the inter-organ scale, C3 operates through a
different mechanism: \emph{bifurcation and low-$K$
relay neurons}.
Neural and vascular trees connect organs of vastly
different impedances by inserting successive branching
levels at progressively lower mode counts $K$.
At each bifurcation, the daughter branch has
$K_{\ell+1} < K_\ell$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:mode-profile}),
creating a staircase of decreasing $K$ that
\emph{translates} high-dimensional cortical signals
into low-dimensional autonomic commands compatible
with peripheral tissue impedances.
The ganglionic relay neurons of the autonomic nervous
system---with $K = 1$--$2$, far below cortical
$K = 5$---are purpose-built C3 translators.
Without these low-$K$ intermediaries, the cortex
could not drive organs whose update physics operates
at $K = 1$ (smooth muscle, secretory epithelium).
Similarly, the vascular tree's Murray bifurcation
(Theorem~\ref{thm:murray}) implements C3 in the
hydraulic domain: each branching level steps
$Z_v$ downward by $n^{4/3}$, grading impedance
from aorta to capillary.

\textbf{Prediction:}
degradation of \emph{any} C3 relay layer produces
pathology proportional to the impedance gap it
previously bridged.
Demyelination (multiple sclerosis): axonal
$\Gamma$ jumps as the myelin relay is removed.
Osteoarthritis: the cartilage relay erodes.
Adventitial fibrosis: perivascular coupling
degrades.
Autonomic neuropathy: loss of low-$K$ relay
neurons causes cortical commands to reflect
at the organ interface.
In each case, the physics is identical:
removing the intermediate $Z$ layer restores
the full $\Gamma = (Z_2-Z_1)/(Z_2+Z_1)$ mismatch.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Thermoregulation}

Blood viscosity: $\tilde\eta(T) = \exp[\alpha(T^{-1}
- T_0^{-1})]$ with $\alpha \approx 1949$~K.
C2 repair:
$\tilde\eta_{\text{learn}}(T) = \exp[\beta_T(T_0^{-1}
- T^{-1})]$ with $\beta_T \approx 6015$~K.
Setting $\tilde\tau(T_c) = \tau_{\max}$:
\begin{equation}
  T_c = \frac{T_0}{1 + T_0\ln\tau_{\max}/
  (\alpha + \beta_T)}.
  \label{eq:Tc}

Higher $K$ raises $T_c$:

the prediction
$\Omega_{\text{plant}} < \Omega_{\text{animal}}$
is testable by cross-kingdom metabolic comparisons.

\section{Discussion}

% ============================================================

\subsection{The concentric-circle structure}

This paper follows a strictly outward derivation:
single vessel $\to$ Murrays Law $\to$ vascular C2 $\to$
dual-network health $\to$ four tissues $\to$
fractal cost $\to$ Kleibers Law.
Each step is a logical consequence of the previous.

\subsection{Timescale separation}

$\eta_v \ll \eta_n$ produces a fast-inner/slow-outer
Tikhonov--Fenichel structure:
neural equilibrates on the fast timescale with
vascular effectively static;
vascular C2 then operates on a pre-adapted neural
background.
Disease manifests when vascular degradation outpaces
neural adaptation (Paper~4~\cite{Paper4}).

\subsection{What ``$1_v \times 1_n = \Omega$'' means}

Each ``1'' is a topologically one-dimensional tree in
3D.
Packing two trees adds $\Acouple > 0$.
The result $\Omega \approx 1.25$--$1.33$ and
$\Omega_n \approx \beta$ suggests:
the cost of adding a second hierarchical tree is of
order one branching level of the first.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Poiseuille (Eq.~\ref{eq:poiseuille}) assumes
    Newtonian fluid, rigid walls; real blood is
    non-Newtonian and vessel walls are viscoelastic.
  \item Organ impedance values in Table~\ref{tab:organs}
    are illustrative, not measured.
  \item The self-similar mode profile
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:mode-profile}) is an idealisation.
  \item $K$ values for neuron types are structural
    observations, not derived from C2.
  \item Capillary invariance is an empirical input.
\end{enumerate}

\begin{remark}[Whole-body C1--C2--C3 simulation:
  thermodynamic hierarchy and trunk criticality]
\label{rem:body-c123-simulation}
A dynamical simulation of 29 organ tissues---each
modelled as a C2 impedance matcher receiving dual
vascular ($Z_v$) and neural ($Z_n$) supply through
a shared C3 relay trunk---confirms the macro-scale
hierarchy:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{C1 (brain)} acts as a homeostatic
    constraint, correcting trunk impedance toward
    organ-demand matches when C2 alone is insufficient.
  \item \textbf{C2 (organs)} performs distributed
    local gradient descent:
    $\Delta Z_k = -\eta\,\Gamma_k\,x_{\text{in},k}\,
    x_{\text{out},k}$ per tissue~$k$.
  \item \textbf{C3 (dual trunk)} relays impedance
    corrections across the vascular and neural buses
    via quarter-wave matching.
\end{itemize}
Whole-body health is multiplicative:
$H = \prod_k (1-\Gamma_{v,k}^2)(1-\Gamma_{n,k}^2)$,
so each tissue's failure cascades through the product.

Four ablation scenarios reveal a key hierarchy:
(i)~full system converges to $H \approx 0.96$;
(ii)~removing C1 (brain offline) degrades health only
mildly in steady state ($H \approx 0.93$);
(iii)~removing C3 (trunk severed) causes catastrophic
collapse ($H \to 0$, all organs starved);
(iv)~aging ($\eta \to 0$) shows gradual decline.
The result that \textbf{No\,C3 $\gg$ No\,C1} in
steady-state damage confirms the thermodynamic hierarchy:
systems whose body thickness satisfies $\ell < \ell^*$
(the diffusion--pump threshold) require no centralised
controller, but \emph{no} system survives trunk loss.

Under acute stress (haemorrhage, surgery, infection),
C1 transitions from redundant to critical:
without C1 correction, haemorrhage drives
$H \approx 0.60$, whereas with C1, $H \approx 0.78$
(\texttt{exp\_body\_stress.py}).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Afferent--efferent asymmetry and the
  directional $K$-staircase]
\label{rem:afferent-efferent-asymmetry}
Sensory (afferent) and motor (efferent) pathways
exhibit a fundamental channel-count asymmetry that
is a direct consequence of the $K$-level structure.

\textbf{Afferent path ($K\!: 1 \to 3 \to 5$, upload).}
A peripheral receptor encodes a stimulus as a single
frequency on a single transmission line.
This signal is progressively combined with others
as it ascends the $K$-hierarchy:
$K = 1$ (raw receptor) $\to$ $K = 3$ (spinal
integration) $\to$ $K = 5$ (cortical
representation).
At each stage, the channel count remains $\leq 1$
per receptor;
the impedance match is hard-wired and does not
require learning.
Hence $\Gamma_{\mathrm{aff}} \to 0$ by construction,
and sensation is immediate from birth.

\textbf{Efferent path ($K\!: 5 \to 3 \to 1$, download).}
A single cortical intention at $K = 5$ must be
\emph{expanded} into $N$ simultaneous output channels
at $K = 1$ (individual motor units):
\[
  1 \;\text{intention} \;\xrightarrow{K=5 \to 3}
  \;\text{motor plan}
  \;\xrightarrow{K=3 \to 1}
  \;N \;\text{muscle fibres.}
\]
Each channel $i$ has its own reflection coefficient
$\Gamma_i$;
the total efferent mismatch is
$\Sigma\Gamma^2_{\mathrm{eff}} =
\sum_{i=1}^{N} \Gamma_i^2$.
Reducing this sum to zero requires simultaneous
C2 gradient descent across all $N$ channels---this
is exactly what \emph{motor learning} accomplishes,
and why skill acquisition takes
$\eta \times n = \text{const}$ repetitions.

\textbf{Bilateral specialisation.}
The anti-parallel $K$-staircase of the two
hemispheres maps onto this asymmetry:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Left hemisphere} ($K\!: 1 \to 5$):
    specialises in single-channel decoding---language
    parsing, sequential logic, analytical detail.
  \item \textbf{Right hemisphere} ($K\!: 5 \to 1$):
    specialises in multi-channel coordination---spatial
    integration, holistic pattern recognition,
    emotional context.
\end{itemize}
Contralateral wiring (left body $\to$ right brain,
right body $\to$ left brain) ensures that afferent
and efferent processing are handled by opposite
hemispheres, maintaining the anti-parallel topology.

\textbf{Clinical predictions:}
(i)~left-hemisphere stroke impairs language
comprehension (single-channel decoder lost);
(ii)~right-hemisphere stroke causes hemispatial
neglect (multi-channel coordinator lost);
(iii)~cerebellar damage preserves sensation and
intention but degrades multi-channel
synchronisation (ataxia);
(iv)~spinal cord injury severs both directions
simultaneously (paralysis + anaesthesia below
the lesion).
\end{remark}
