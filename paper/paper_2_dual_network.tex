\begin{abstract}
We ask: given a blood vessel with impedance
$\Zv = 8\mu L/\pi r^4$ governed by vascular C2 remodeling,
what architecture---and what metabolic scaling laws---must
emerge?

\textbf{Part~A} derives vascular architecture from the
Minimum Reflection Principle (MRP).
A blood vessel is a hydraulic transmission line.
At every bifurcation, impedance mismatch produces a
reflection $\Gv$; C1 gives $\Gv^2 + \Tv = 1$.
We derive Murray's Law ($r_p^3 = \sum r_i^3$) as the
exact minimum of the vascular action
$\Av = \sum_j[Q_j^2 Z_j + \lambda V_j]$, with 100\%
numerical agreement for $n=2,3,4$.
Vascular C2 remodeling ($\Delta\Zv=-\eta_v\Gv\tau_{\text{wall}}\rho$)
operates on the same principle as neural C2 but with
$\eta_v \ll \eta_n$.

\textbf{Part~B} derives the dual-network organ model.
Every organ is served by two coincident networks:
vascular ($\Gv$) and neural ($\Gn$).
Organ health $H = \Tn\cdot\Tv = (1-\Gn^2)(1-\Gv^2)$
follows from multiplicative transmission.
Four failure modes are classified by which network fails.
The organ impedance hierarchy ranks ten organs by their
resting $\Gv^2$.

\textbf{Part~C} derives metabolic scaling.
A self-similar relay hierarchy with branching number~$n$
in $d$ dimensions produces an irreducible action
$\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}$ (for $d=3$: $M^{2/3}$).
The Murray bifurcation ratio $\beta = n^{1/d} = 2^{1/3}$
sets length and radius contraction across levels.
Kleiber's Law ($B \propto M^{3/4}$) follows from
capillary invariance.
Dual-network packing yields a metabolic overhead
$\Omega \approx 1.33$, with $\Omega_n \approx \beta$.
Multi-scale verification confirms the irreducibility
theorem across cardiovascular, musculoskeletal, and
29-tissue-atlas scales.

\textbf{Part~D} demonstrates interface universality.
The same $\Gamma=(Z_2-Z_1)/(Z_2+Z_1)$ governs seven
physical domains across 14 orders of magnitude---from
synapses to power grids.
The Interface Isomorphism Proposition proves that $\Gamma$
depends only on boundary impedance, not bulk properties.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
% ============================================================

A blood vessel is a tube carrying fluid under pressure.
Its impedance---the ratio of pressure drop to volume
flow---is a physical fact set by geometry and fluid
properties.

Paper~1~\cite{Paper1} derives neural architecture and
the mind from the Minimum Reflection Principle (MRP).
Yet every organ is governed by a \emph{second} impedance
network: the vasculature.
The pressure wave in an artery reflects at every
bifurcation, stricture, or compliance mismatch,
precisely as an electromagnetic wave reflects at a
transmission-line discontinuity~\cite{nichols2011,
womersley1955}.

This paper derives the vascular architecture from the
same MRP, then combines both networks into the
dual-network organ model, and finally shows that
metabolic scaling laws (Murray, Kleiber) are necessary
consequences of the same irreducible topology.

The derivation follows a strictly outward chain:
single vessel (Sec.~\ref{sec:framework}) $\to$
Murray's Law (Sec.~\ref{sec:murray}) $\to$
vascular C2 (Sec.~\ref{sec:vascular-C2}) $\to$
dual-network organ model (Sec.~\ref{sec:organ-model})
$\to$ fractal cost (Sec.~\ref{sec:fractal-cost}) $\to$
Kleiber's Law (Sec.~\ref{sec:murray-kleiber}) $\to$
interface universality (Sec.~\ref{sec:scale}).

\section{Vascular Impedance Physics}
\label{sec:framework}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The blood vessel as transmission line}

A cylindrical vessel of length~$L$ and radius~$r$
carrying a Newtonian fluid of viscosity~$\mu$ has
steady-state impedance given by the Poiseuille law:
\begin{equation}
  \Zv = \frac{\Delta P}{Q} = \frac{8\mu L}{\pi r^4}.
  \label{eq:poiseuille}
\end{equation}

In the pulsatile regime, Womersley theory adds
a frequency-dependent component:
\begin{equation}
  \Zv(\omega) = \frac{8\mu L}{\pi r^4}
    \cdot F(\alpha),
  \label{eq:womersley}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = r\sqrt{\omega\rho_f/\mu}$ is the
Womersley number, $F(\alpha) \to 1$ for
$\alpha \ll 1$ (Poiseuille limit) and
$F(\alpha) \to j\alpha^2/8$ for $\alpha \gg 1$
(inertial limit)~\cite{womersley1955}.

\subsection{Three physical laws in the vascular domain}
\label{sec:vascular-laws}

\textbf{C1---Energy Conservation.}
$\Gv^2 + \Tv = 1$ at every vascular junction.

\textbf{C2---Vascular Remodeling.}
\begin{equation}
  \Delta\Zv = -\eta_v\,\Gv\,\tau_{\text{wall}}\,\rho,
  \label{eq:C2v}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_{\text{wall}}$ is wall shear stress
($x_{\text{in}}$), $\rho$ is material supply
($x_{\text{out}}$), and $\eta_v$ is the vascular
remodeling rate.

\textbf{C3---Dimensional Relay.}
When vascular and neural $K$-values differ,
relay nodes (e.g.\ baroreceptors reading
vessel-wall pulse density) bridge the
dimensional gap.

\begin{remark}[Pulse density as real-time $Z$
  encoding \textnormal{(E1)}]
\label{rem:pulse-density}
Two C3 relay examples make the mechanism
concrete:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Baroreceptors} fire at a rate
    proportional to vessel-wall strain, i.e.\
    their firing frequency encodes the local
    vascular impedance $Z_v$ in real time.
    The brainstem reads this pulse density
    to regulate cardiac output---a
    dimensional relay from mechanical ($Z_v$)
    to neural ($Z_n$) via frequency modulation.

  \item \textbf{Bone piezoelectricity}
    (Wolff's Law~\cite{Wolff1892}):
    mechanical loading generates charge
    proportional to strain, producing action
    potentials in periosteal neurons.
    Pulse density again encodes local $Z$,
    bridging the mechanical--electrical
    dimensional gap.
\end{enumerate}

\noindent
\textbf{Hypertension as chronic $\Gamma$ compensation.}
If a tissue sustains elevated $\Gamma^2$
(chronic mismatch), C2 cannot resolve it
locally.
The baroreceptor relay reports the persistent
impedance deviation to the brainstem, which
responds by raising cardiac output to that
vascular territory.
If the mismatch never resolves,
the brain \emph{continuously forces the heart
to over-pump}---producing sustained
hypertension not as a disease but as a
C2/C3 feedback response to an
unresolved $\Gamma$ source.
\textbf{Falsifiable prediction:}
mapping the spatial $\Gamma^2$ profile via
pulse-wave velocity should identify the
tissue origin of ``essential'' hypertension.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Bifurcation reflection}
\label{sec:bifurcation}

At a symmetric bifurcation ($n$ identical daughters),
$\Zv^{\text{down}} = \Zv^d / n$:
\begin{equation}
  \Gv = \frac{\Zv^d/n - \Zv^p}{\Zv^d/n + \Zv^p}.
  \label{eq:bif-gamma}
\end{equation}
Pure impedance matching ($\Gv = 0$) gives
$r_d = r_p / n^{1/4}$, which \emph{disagrees} with
Murray's Law.
The resolution: MRP minimises the \emph{total vascular
action} including metabolic maintenance cost.

\begin{remark}[Why impedance disparity is extreme \textnormal{(E1)}]
\label{rem:impedance-disparity}
Characteristic impedance $Z$ depends on the physical
properties of the medium---density, elastic modulus,
membrane resistance---not on any single variable.
In the human body, these properties span orders of
magnitude:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Cortical neurons}: high membrane
    resistance ($R_m \sim 10^4\;\Omega\,\mathrm{cm}^2$),
    giving high characteristic impedance $Z_n$;
  \item \emph{Skeletal muscle}: low membrane resistance,
    high density ($\rho \sim 1.06\;\mathrm{g/cm^3}$),
    giving low characteristic impedance $Z_m \ll Z_n$;
  \item \emph{Cortical bone}: elastic modulus
    $E \sim 18\;\mathrm{GPa}$, two orders above
    soft tissue ($\sim 10\;\mathrm{kPa}$).
\end{itemize}
Direct connection between any two such media gives
$\Gamma \to 1$ (total reflection).
Multi-stage fractal matching---the vascular tree
for hydraulic impedance, the neural relay hierarchy
for electromagnetic impedance---is therefore not merely
optimal but \emph{physically necessary}.
Murray's Law (below) quantifies the exact geometry
of the vascular solution.
\end{remark}

\section{Murray's Law from the Vascular Action Principle}
\label{sec:murray}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Vascular action functional}

\begin{equation}
  \Av = \sum_j \bigl[Q_j^2\,Z_j + \lambda\,V_j\bigr],
  \label{eq:vascular-action}
\end{equation}
where $Q_j$ is flow, $Z_j = 8\mu L_j/\pi r_j^4$
is impedance, $V_j = \pi r_j^2 L_j$ is volume, and
$\lambda$ is the metabolic cost per unit volume.

\subsection{Derivation for symmetric $n$-branching}

Daughter contribution:
\begin{equation}
  \Av^d = \frac{Q^2\,8\mu L_d}{n\,\pi\,r_d^4}
    + n\lambda\pi r_d^2 L_d.
  \label{eq:Av-daughter}
\end{equation}
Minimising $\partial\Av^d/\partial r_d = 0$:
\begin{equation}
  r_d^6 = \frac{16\,Q^2\,\mu}{n^2\,\lambda\,\pi^2},
  \qquad
  r_p^6 = \frac{16\,Q^2\,\mu}{\lambda\,\pi^2}.
  \label{eq:rd-rp-opt}
\end{equation}
Taking the ratio:

\begin{theorem}[Murray's Law from MRP \textnormal{(E0)}]
\label{thm:murray}
For symmetric $n$-branching of a vessel with parent
radius~$r_p$, minimisation of the vascular action
$\Av = \sum_j [Q_j^2 Z_j + \lambda V_j]$ yields
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{r_d = \frac{r_p}{n^{1/3}},}
  \label{eq:murray}
\end{equation}
equivalently $r_p^3 = \sum_{i=1}^n r_{d,i}^3$
(the cube law).
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Setting $\partial\Av^d/\partial r_d = 0$ in
Eq.~\eqref{eq:Av-daughter} gives
$r_d^6 = 16 Q^2 \mu / (n^2 \lambda \pi^2)$ and
$r_p^6 = 16 Q^2 \mu / (\lambda \pi^2)$.
The ratio $r_d^6/r_p^6 = 1/n^2$ gives
$r_d/r_p = n^{-1/3}$.
The $1/3$ exponent---rather than $1/4$ from pure
impedance matching---arises from the balance of
dissipation ($\propto r^{-4}$) and maintenance
($\propto r^{+2}$).
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Physics, not optimisation \textnormal{(E1)}]
The $1/3$ exponent arises from the balance of dissipation
($\propto r^{-4}$) and maintenance ($\propto r^{+2}$).
Pure impedance matching gives $1/4$; MRP gives $1/3$.
Murray's Law is a physical law of impedance networks.
\end{remark}

\section{Organ Health from Multiplicative Transmission}
\label{sec:organ-model}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Why two networks}

Neural C2 requires material substrate ($\rho$) from
vascular delivery.
Vascular C2 requires neural control (autonomic signals
regulate vascular tone).
The two networks are \emph{mutually prerequisite}.

\begin{remark}[Blood as crosstalk isolator \textnormal{(E1)}]
\label{rem:blood-isolation}
In microwave circuits, absorbing material is placed
between adjacent channels to prevent reflection
pulses in one channel from contaminating another
(crosstalk isolation).
Blood serves the same function:  when a neural
interface produces a reflection pulse
$\Gamma_i^2 P_{\text{in},i}$,
part of the scattered energy propagates laterally.
Blood, with its own characteristic impedance
$Z_{\text{blood}}$, absorbs this stray energy and
converts it to heat, which is carried away by
convective flow (Eq.~\ref{eq:thermal-bus}).
This yields a third reason for the dual-network
architecture beyond mutual prerequisiteness and
thermal regulation:
\emph{impedance isolation between densely packed
neural interfaces}.
The blood--brain barrier is, in this view,
an impedance wall that prevents vascular $Z$
fluctuations from polluting neural $Z$;
cerebrospinal fluid is an impedance-matched
filler between brain and cranium.
The brain's disproportionate share of cardiac
output ($\sim$15\%) is driven not solely by oxygen
demand but by the crosstalk-isolation requirements
of its extremely dense relay architecture.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Organ health}

At each organ, the neural network transmits $\Tn = 1-\Gn^2$
and the vascular network transmits $\Tv = 1-\Gv^2$.
Series connection:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{H = \Tn \cdot \Tv = (1-\Gn^2)(1-\Gv^2).}
  \label{eq:dual-health}
\end{equation}
$H=1$: perfect transmission; $H=0$: at least one
network totally blocked.

\subsection{Thermal bus}

The vascular network carries heat:
\begin{equation}
  \dot{Q}_{\text{organ}} = c_p\,\rho_f\,Q
    \,(T_{\text{art}} - T_{\text{ven}}).
  \label{eq:thermal-bus}
\end{equation}
When $\Gv^2$ increases, $Q$ decreases and thermal
exchange is compromised.

\subsection{Four irreducible tissue types}
\label{sec:four-tissues}

C2 has four prerequisites:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Signal} ($x_{\text{in}}$) to detect
    mismatch $\Gamma$ $\to$ \textbf{Nervous tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Material substrate} ($x_{\text{out}}$)
    delivered by pipe network $\to$
    \textbf{Connective (vascular) tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Mechanical actuator} to apply force
    for structural update $\to$ \textbf{Muscle tissue}.
  \item \textbf{Boundary} defining spatial domain of $Z$
    measurement $\to$ \textbf{Epithelial tissue}.
\end{enumerate}
The dependency cycle is closed:
\[
  \text{Nervous} \xrightarrow{\text{needs }\rho}
  \text{Connective} \xrightarrow{\text{needs pump}}
  \text{Muscle} \xrightarrow{\text{needs boundary}}
  \text{Epithelial} \xrightarrow{\text{needs control}}
  \text{Nervous}.
\]
Removing any one tissue collapses C2.
\textbf{Four is both necessary and sufficient for C2
viability.}

\section{Irreducible Action of a Self-Similar Relay Hierarchy}
\label{sec:fractal-cost}
% ============================================================

Paper~1~\cite{Paper1} proved $\Acut > 0$ (the
Dimensional Cost Irreducibility Theorem) but left its
geometric structure unspecified.
This section asks: \emph{how big} is $\Acut$?

\subsection{Mode-count profile}

Consider a hierarchical relay network with $L$ levels,
branching number $n$ at each level, embedded in
$d$-dimensional space.
Level~0 is the root (aorta, cortex); level~$L$ the
terminal (capillary, sensory ending).
At level~$\ell$: $N_\ell = n^\ell$ nodes,
$l_\ell = l_0\,n^{-\ell/d}$ (space-filling), and
mode count:
\begin{equation}
  K_\ell = K_0\,c^\ell, \quad 0 < c < 1,
  \label{eq:mode-profile}
\end{equation}
with $c = n^{-1/d}$ when the cutoff is set by branch
length.

\subsection{$\Acut$ at each level}

From the Irreducibility Theorem (Paper~1,
Theorem~1~\cite{Paper1}), at level~$\ell$ there are
$N_\ell = n^\ell$ edges, each contributing
$(K_\ell - K_{\ell+1})^+$:
\begin{equation}
  \Acut = K_0(1 - c) \sum_{\ell=0}^{L-1} (nc)^\ell.
  \label{eq:acut-sum}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Scaling law}

With $c = n^{-1/d}$, the base $nc = n^{(d-1)/d} > 1$
for $d \ge 2$, so the sum is dominated by the last
term:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\Acut \propto M^{(d-1)/d}.}
  \label{eq:acut-mass}
\end{equation}
\textbf{For $d = 3$}: $\Acut \propto M^{2/3}$.
The per-cell cost $a_{\text{cut}} = \Acut/M \propto
M^{-1/3}$ \emph{decreases} with organism size---economies
of scale from the relay hierarchy.

\section{Murray's Ratio and Metabolic Scaling}
\label{sec:murray-kleiber}
% ============================================================

\subsection{The Murray bifurcation ratio}

Each branching level contracts the service length by
$l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
The bifurcation ratio:
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\beta \equiv n^{1/d}.}
  \label{eq:beta-def}
\end{equation}
For binary bifurcation ($n=2$) in $d=3$:
$\beta = 2^{1/3} = 1.2599\ldots$
From Murray's Law (Eq.~\ref{eq:murray}):
$r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell/n^{1/3}$; for $d=3$,
$r_{\ell+1} = r_\ell/\beta$.

\subsection{Network maintenance cost}

Relay reflection power:
$P_{\text{reflect}} = \Acut \cdot P_0/N_T
\propto M^{(d-1)/d} \cdot M^{-1} = M^{-1/d}$.
Total network dissipation:
\begin{equation}
  B_{\text{network}} \propto M \cdot M^{-1/d}
  = M^{(d-1)/d}.
  \label{eq:B-network}
\end{equation}
For $d=3$: $B_{\text{network}} \propto M^{2/3}$.

\begin{theorem}[Kleiber's Law from MRP \textnormal{(E0)}]
\label{thm:kleiber}
For a self-similar vascular tree with branching number~$n$
in $d$ spatial dimensions, MRP implies
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{B \propto M^{d/(d+1)}.}
  \label{eq:kleiber}
\end{equation}
For $d=3$: $B \propto M^{3/4}$---Kleiber's
Law~\cite{Kleiber1932}.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
Murray's Law (Theorem~\ref{thm:murray}) gives
$r_{\ell+1}/r_\ell = n^{-1/3}$.
Space-filling determines the length ratio
$l_{\ell+1}/l_\ell = n^{-1/d}$.
Capillary invariance fixes the terminal scale;
blood volume $\propto M$.
Combining: the number of terminal units is
$N_T \propto M^{d/(d+1)}$, and since each terminal
unit has a fixed metabolic rate, $B \propto N_T
\propto M^{d/(d+1)}$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Volumetric heat density}

Every active interface dissipates $\Gamma_i^2 P_{\text{in},i}$
as heat (Paper~0, Corollary~1~\cite{Paper0}).
A tissue with $K$ modes:
\begin{equation}
  q \propto K \cdot \avg{\Gamma^2} \cdot P_{\text{in}}
  / V_{\text{node}}.
  \label{eq:vol-heat}
\end{equation}
The human brain ($K=5$) is $\sim$2\% of body mass but
$\sim$25\% of basal metabolic heat---because its
per-node mode count is largest.

\section{Dual-Network Packing}
\label{sec:dual-packing}
% ============================================================

\subsection{Two trees in one volume}

A multicellular organism requires two space-filling
networks: the \textbf{vascular tree}
($Z_v = \Delta P/Q$, transport) and the
\textbf{neural tree}
($Z_n = R_m + 1/j\omega C_m$, control).

\begin{theorem}[Dual-Network Overhead \textnormal{(E0)}]
\label{thm:packing}
When two space-filling relay trees share a volume in
$d$~dimensions,
\begin{equation}
  \Acut^{\text{dual}} = \Acut^v + \Acut^n + \Acouple,
  \quad \Acouple > 0.
  \label{eq:acut-dual}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
At co-terminal points, the two networks negotiate
resource allocation.
The coupling mismatch
$\Gamma_{\text{couple}} =
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} - Z_v^{\text{supply}}) /
(Z_n^{\text{demand}} + Z_v^{\text{supply}})$
is generically nonzero because each tree optimises
independently.
Hence $\Acouple = N_T \avg{\Gamma_{\text{couple}}^2} > 0$.
\end{proof}

\subsection{The metabolic overhead factor}

\begin{equation}
  \Omega \equiv \frac{B_{\text{total}}}{B_{\text{tissue}}}
  = 1 + \frac{B_{\text{vasc}} + B_{\text{neural}}}
             {B_{\text{tissue}}}.
  \label{eq:omega-def}
\end{equation}
For humans:
$B_{\text{neural}}/B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.20$;
$B_{\text{vasc}}/B_{\text{total}} \approx 0.05$.
Therefore $\Omega \approx 1/0.75 \approx 1.33$.
The neural network alone gives
$\Omega_n = 1/(1-0.20) = 1.25 \approx \beta = 2^{1/3}$.

\begin{remark}[Organ-specific $\Omega_i$:
  bottom-up recovery of $\tfrac{4}{3}$ \textnormal{(E1)}]
\label{rem:omega-organ}
The global $\Omega$ can be decomposed into
organ-specific overhead factors.
For each organ $i$, define
$\Omega_i = 1 + \alpha\,
\mathrm{BF}_i \times \mathrm{NI}_i / 5$,
where $\mathrm{BF}_i$ is the blood-flow fraction
(Guyton Table~17-1) and $\mathrm{NI}_i \in \{1,\ldots,5\}$
is the neural innervation density
(autonomic + somatic).
Paper~5 computes:
\[
  \overline{\Omega_i}
  = \frac{1}{12}\sum_{i=1}^{12} \Omega_i
  = 1.376
  \approx \Omega_{\text{global}} = 1.33.
\]
The $\sim\!3\%$ agreement between the
bottom-up organ average and the top-down
whole-body value constitutes an independent
consistency check.
The highest-overhead organs are
neuro ($\Omega = 2.00$),
renal ($1.88$), and
GI ($1.80$);
the lowest are
endocrine ($1.04$) and
reproductive ($1.05$).
\end{remark}

\subsection{Why blood before nerve}

C2 requires a carrier for $x_{\text{in}}$:
neurotransmitter synthesis needs glucose and oxygen from
blood.
Without the vascular network, $x_{\text{in}} = 0$,
$\Delta Z = 0$: the neural network cannot remodel.
Embryology confirms: cardiac tube at day~21; neural tube
closure at day~25--28.
In the product $1_v \times 1_n$, the vascular ``1'' is
the left factor.

\subsection{Twenty-nine tissue atlas}

A comprehensive atlas of 29~organ-system blueprints

All 29 verified:  C1 holds, $\Aimp$ non-increasing,
$\Acut$ topologically invariant.

(\texttt{tests/test\_tissue\_blueprints.py}).

\subsection{Whole-body inter-organ topology}

Connecting the 29~blueprints requires:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Vascular bus}: aortic trunk ($K=4$)
    $\to$ 29 arteriolar feeds ($K=2$),
    $\Acut = 2$ per edge.
  \item \textbf{Neural bus}: autonomic trunk ($K=3$)
    $\to$ 29 nerve feeds ($K=2$),
    $\Acut = 1$ per edge.
  \item \textbf{Brain hub}: hypothalamus ($K=3$)
    drives the neural bus; the vascular bus feeds
    signals back to thalamus ($K=5$).
\end{enumerate}
Unified whole-body: 278~nodes, 426~edges,
$\Acut = 222$ (the structural information cost of
multi-organ life).

\subsection{Thermoregulation}

Blood viscosity: $\tilde\eta(T) = \exp[\alpha(T^{-1}
- T_0^{-1})]$ with $\alpha \approx 1949$~K.
C2 repair:
$\tilde\eta_{\text{learn}}(T) = \exp[\beta_T(T_0^{-1}
- T^{-1})]$ with $\beta_T \approx 6015$~K.
Setting $\tilde\tau(T_c) = \tau_{\max}$:
\begin{equation}
  T_c = \frac{T_0}{1 + T_0\ln\tau_{\max}/
  (\alpha + \beta_T)}.
  \label{eq:Tc}
\end{equation}
Higher $K$ raises $T_c$:
$T_c(K{=}1) = -10.3\,^\circ$C (ectotherm viable);
$T_c(K{=}5) = +4.5\,^\circ$C (endothermy required).

\subsection{Geometric universality}

$\beta = n^{1/d}$ is a geometric inevitability for any

substrate.

$\beta$ is a property of geometry, not biology.
Plants have $\Omega \approx 1$ (no neural network);
the prediction
$\Omega_{\text{plant}} < \Omega_{\text{animal}}$
is testable by cross-kingdom metabolic comparisons.

\section{Interface Isomorphism}
\label{sec:isomorphism}
% ============================================================

\begin{proposition}[Interface Isomorphism]
\label{prop:isomorphism}
The reflection coefficient $\Gamma$ at any interface
depends only on the impedance contrast at the boundary,
not on the bulk properties of either medium.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The formula $\Gamma = (Z_2-Z_1)/(Z_2+Z_1)$ contains
only boundary values $Z_1$ and $Z_2$.
The derivation uses only continuity conditions at the
interface and conservation of power.
No property of the bulk medium enters the result.
\end{proof}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{The concentric-circle structure}

This paper follows a strictly outward derivation:
single vessel $\to$ Murray's Law $\to$ vascular C2 $\to$
dual-network health $\to$ four tissues $\to$
fractal cost $\to$ Kleiber's Law $\to$ interface
universality.
Each step is a logical consequence of the previous.

\subsection{Timescale separation}

$\eta_v \ll \eta_n$ produces a fast-inner/slow-outer
Tikhonov--Fenichel structure:
neural equilibrates on the fast timescale with
vascular effectively static;
vascular C2 then operates on a pre-adapted neural
background.
Disease manifests when vascular degradation outpaces
neural adaptation (Paper~4~\cite{Paper4}).

\subsection{What ``$1_v \times 1_n = \Omega$'' means}

Each ``1'' is a topologically one-dimensional tree in
3D.
Packing two trees adds $\Acouple > 0$.
The result $\Omega \approx 1.25$--$1.33$ and
$\Omega_n \approx \beta$ suggests:
the cost of adding a second hierarchical tree is of
order one branching level of the first.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Poiseuille (Eq.~\ref{eq:poiseuille}) assumes
    Newtonian fluid, rigid walls; real blood is
    non-Newtonian and vessel walls are viscoelastic.
  \item Organ impedance values in Table~\ref{tab:organs}
    are illustrative, not measured.
  \item The self-similar mode profile
    (Eq.~\ref{eq:mode-profile}) is an idealisation.
  \item $K$ values for neuron types are structural
    observations, not derived from C2.
  \item Capillary invariance is an empirical input.
\end{enumerate}
